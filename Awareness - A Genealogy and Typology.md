# "Awareness" / 気づき: A Genealogy and Typology of a Semantically Overloaded Term

## Introduction

Few words in contemporary English-language spirituality and psychology do as much work — and conceal as much confusion — as the word *awareness*. In Buddhist popular literature, in clinical mindfulness manuals, in coaching scripts, in psychedelic discourse, in self-help and corporate wellness, the word floats free of any single referent. Its Japanese counterpart, 気づき (*kizuki*), shows the same pattern: a polysemous ordinary-language noun pressed into service as the umbrella translation for at least a dozen technically distinct Buddhist terms and several non-Buddhist therapeutic constructs.

This report has three aims. First, to lay out the Buddhist technical vocabulary that is collapsed into the single English word *awareness* (or 気づき in Japanese), with each term's Abhidharma definition. Second, to trace the historical channels — Orientalist scholarship, Theravāda reform movements, the counterculture, the Human Potential Movement, clinical mindfulness, and the contemporary digital wellness industry — through which the word acquired its present semantic inflation. Third, to typologize the conceptual confusions that this inflation generates, and to spell out why those confusions matter for both practice and interpretation.

## PART 1 — Buddhist Technical Terms Collapsed Into "Awareness" / 気づき

### 1.1 *Sati* / *smṛti* (念)

*Sati* (Pāli; Sanskrit *smṛti*; Chinese/Japanese 念 *nen*) is the term that became "mindfulness" in English and has, in the last twenty years, drifted further toward "awareness." Its semantic field in Indic languages is unusually wide. Rupert Gethin, in his 2011 article "On Some Definitions of Mindfulness" (*Contemporary Buddhism* 12:1), shows that the Pāli Abhidhamma (in particular the *Dhammasaṅgaṇi* and its commentary the *Atthasālinī*) defines *sati* via a cluster of synonyms — *anussati* (recollection), *paṭissati* (recall), *saraṇatā* (remembrance), *dhāraṇatā* (bearing in mind), *apilāpanatā* (non-floating, i.e. not letting the object slip away), *asammussanatā* (non-forgetfulness). Asaṅga's *Abhidharmasamuccaya* gives the same core gloss: "non-forgetfulness of mind with regard to the object of experience." Vasubandhu's *Abhidharmakośa* describes *smṛty-upasthāna* in terms of "watching the body in the body," where the watching itself is wisdom (*prajñā*) supported by the "wedge" of *smṛti*.

Crucially, *sati* in this Abhidharma sense is **not** "bare attention" and **not** "present-moment awareness" as such. It is a specific *cetasika* (mental factor) whose function (*rasa*) is *apilāpana* — keeping the object from drifting away — and whose proximate cause is *thirasaññā*, firm perception. Its scriptural metaphor is the gatekeeper who recognizes who may and may not enter the city: a *recognitional, recollective* function tied to ethical discrimination, not merely a register of present sensation.

T. W. Rhys Davids's 1881 translation of *sati* as "mindfulness" — chosen, Gethin notes, without explanation in any of his prefaces — pulled away from the dictionary equivalents available to him (Monier-Williams 1872 gives *smṛti* as "remembrance, memory, reminiscence, recollection, calling to mind"). "Mindfulness" was an archaic English word, then little used, that Rhys Davids reactivated to gesture toward a quality of attentive presence; in doing so he detached the term from its dominant Indic meaning of *memory*. From the 1920s through the 1950s, "mindfulness" became the standard English rendering, propagated by Soma Thera (*The Way of Mindfulness*, 1941), Nyanaponika, and later by Thich Nhat Hanh.

What is lost in the slide from *sati* to "awareness": (1) the recollective dimension, which links present perception to remembered teaching and to a moral framework — Bhikkhu Anālayo, in *Satipaṭṭhāna: The Direct Path to Realization* (Windhorse, 2003), and Georges Dreyfus have both insisted that the *memory* sense is constitutive, not optional; (2) the active, effortful character (*sati* in the *satta bojjhaṅgā* is the first factor *because* it must be aroused, not because it is a ground state); (3) the ethical directionality — *sammā-sati* (right mindfulness) is the seventh limb of the eightfold path and is defined as the four *satipaṭṭhānas*, not as content-neutral attention.

### 1.2 *Sampajañña* / *samprajanya* (正知)

*Sampajañña* (Sanskrit *samprajanya*; Chinese 正知 *zhèngzhī*; Japanese 正知 *shōchi*) is "clear comprehension" or "clearly knowing." The Pāli commentaries (*Atthasālinī*, *Visuddhimagga*) analyze it under four headings: *sātthaka-sampajañña* (clear comprehension of *purpose* — is this action conducive to the path?), *sappāya-sampajañña* (of *suitability* — is it appropriate here and now?), *gocara-sampajañña* (of the *meditative domain* — keeping within one's object), and *asammoha-sampajañña* (of *non-delusion* — seeing impermanence, suffering, and non-self in the activity).

In the *Satipaṭṭhāna Sutta* (DN 22, MN 10), the formula is invariably *ātāpī sampajāno satimā* — "ardent, clearly comprehending, and mindful." *Sati* and *sampajañña* are paired but distinct: *sati* keeps the object in view; *sampajañña* knows what is going on, why, and whether it is wholesome. Bhikkhu Bodhi, in his published correspondence with Alan Wallace, makes the point that even Nyanaponika Thera himself did not regard "bare attention" as exhausting *satipaṭṭhāna*; it was only "the initial phase," and right mindfulness required *sati* and *sampajañña* working in tandem.

The damage of collapsing *sampajañña* into "awareness" or even into "mindfulness" is that the metacognitive, evaluative, ethical-discriminative function of *sampajañña* disappears. Bare attention to a sensation is precisely **not** *sampajañña*; *sampajañña* asks whether the activity is *worth doing*, whether it accords with the path, whether it perceives anicca. The Vipassana Research Institute (Goenka lineage) translates the term as "constant thorough understanding of impermanence" specifically to push back against the reduction to mere observation.

### 1.3 *Manasikāra* (作意)

*Manasikāra* (Sanskrit identical; Chinese/Japanese 作意 *sa'i*; Tibetan *yid la byed pa*) literally means "making in the mind," and is standardly rendered "attention" or "mental advertence." In the Theravāda Abhidhamma it is one of the *seven universal mental factors* (*sabbacittasādhāraṇa cetasika*) — present in every single moment of consciousness without exception. In the Yogācāra *Pañcaskandhaprakaraṇa* and *Abhidharmasamuccaya* it is one of the five omnipresent factors (*sarvatraga*). Its function is *to direct or yoke the mind to the object*; Buddhaghosa's metaphor is the rudder of a ship or the charioteer who steers the horses (associated states) toward the destination (object). Without *manasikāra*, an object cannot be "made present" to consciousness at all.

Manasikāra is therefore **ethically neutral** and **universal** — it is present in greed-rooted consciousness as much as in wisdom-rooted consciousness. This is why the canonical literature distinguishes *yoniso manasikāra* (wise attention) from *ayoniso manasikāra* (unwise attention): the factor itself is neutral; what matters is its mode.

Jonardon Ganeri (*Attention, Not Self*, Oxford 2017) and Bhikkhu Bodhi both insist that conflating *manasikāra* with *sati* obliterates the Abhidhamma's most basic distinction. *Manasikāra* is the universal directing-to-object; *sati* is a specifically wholesome (*sobhana*) factor present only in skillful consciousness. When Jon Kabat-Zinn defines mindfulness as "paying attention in a particular way: on purpose, in the present moment, and nonjudgmentally," he is describing a structure closer to *yoniso manasikāra* than to *sati* in the technical Abhidhamma sense. The English word "awareness" then absorbs both, erasing the difference.

### 1.4 *Āvajjana* (転向)

In the Theravāda *citta-vīthi* (cognitive process theory) elaborated in the *Abhidhammatthasaṅgaha*, *āvajjana* names the "adverting" function — the moment of mind at which a stimulus interrupts the *bhavaṅga* (life-continuum) and consciousness "turns toward" the new object. There are two: *pañcadvārāvajjana* (adverting at the five sense-doors) and *manodvārāvajjana* (adverting at the mind-door). These are sub-personal, sub-reflective moments occurring in nanoseconds; the *bhavaṅga* is itself unconscious, and *āvajjana* is the very first flicker of object-presentation.

Popular "awareness" discourse, when it borrows the suggestive language of "noticing" or "orienting," frequently collapses this fine-grained processual analysis into a single phenomenological category. But *āvajjana* is not introspectively accessible in the way "awareness" is, and treating it as such — as some neuro-mindfulness literature does when it equates "the moment of noticing" with the meditative goal — distorts a technical processual term into a romanticized epiphany.

### 1.5 *Viññāṇa* / *vijñāna* (識)

*Viññāṇa* (Sanskrit *vijñāna*; Chinese/Japanese 識 *shiki*) is "consciousness" in the most basic sense — the bare cognizing of an object. The Pāli suttas distinguish six *viññāṇas* corresponding to the six sense-doors (eye-, ear-, nose-, tongue-, body-, and mind-consciousness). It is the fifth *khandha* and a member of the twelve-link chain of dependent origination.

*Viññāṇa* is *not* reflective awareness, not introspection, not metacognition. It is the bare presence of an object to a sense faculty. In Western philosophical terms it is closer to "phenomenal consciousness" or "raw cognizing capacity" than to "awareness" in the self-help sense. The Yogācāra tradition elaborates eight *vijñānas*, including the *ālaya-vijñāna* (store-consciousness) and *kliṣṭa-manas* (afflicted ego-consciousness) — and even here the term names a functional level of cognizing, not a state cultivated by practice.

When "awareness" is used to translate both *sati* and *viññāṇa*, the practitioner cannot tell whether they are being asked to *cultivate* a trainable factor or to *recognize* a structural feature of every cognitive moment. The two ask for entirely different things.

### 1.6 *Paññā* / *prajñā* (慧)

*Paññā* (Sanskrit *prajñā*; Chinese/Japanese 慧 *e* / *chi-e*) is "wisdom" or "discernment." In the Pāli Abhidhamma it is the cetasika of *amoha* (non-delusion), defined as the seeing of phenomena according to their actual nature (*yathābhūta*). Vasubandhu, as Gethin notes, takes the position in the *Abhidharmakośa* that *smṛty-upasthāna* is "from the point of view of essential nature, *prajñā*" — *sati* is the support; the seeing itself is *paññā*.

In Mahāmudrā and Dzogchen contexts, *prajñā* (Tib. *shes rab*) is sometimes used interchangeably with *rigpa* in loose talk, even though the technical Tibetan literature carefully distinguishes them (see 1.7). The result, in English: *paññā*, *prajñā*, *jñāna*, *vidyā*, *rigpa*, and *shes rab* all get translated at various times as "awareness," "knowledge," "wisdom," or "insight," and the reader is given no way to tell which technical term lies behind any given English word.

### 1.7 *Rigpa* (Tibetan *rig pa*, Sanskrit *vidyā*)

*Rigpa* is the central term of the Dzogchen ("Great Perfection") tradition of the Tibetan Nyingma school. It translates Sanskrit *vidyā*, "knowledge" — but in the Dzogchen technical lexicon it specifies "pure awareness" or "pristine cognition," the innate, unfabricated, non-dual reflexive knowing that is the *nature* of mind (as opposed to *sems*, ordinary dualistic mind). The Dalai Lama, in his Dzogchen teachings, formulates the standard distinction: *sem* is mind insofar as it is obscured by subject-object thought; *rigpa* is mind insofar as it is pure, non-dual, free of elaboration. Jikmé Lingpa, in his *Treasury of Precious Qualities*, says that *rigpa* "transcends the ordinary mind (citta)" and is the "special feature of the natural Dzogpachenpo." Sam van Schaik translates *rigpa* as "gnosis," glossed as "a form of awareness aligned to the nirvanic state, free from all delusion," and notes that other Tibetan glosses include *spros bral* ("free from elaborations"), *rtog med* ("nonconceptual"), and *blo 'das* ("beyond intellect").

The mass popularization of *rigpa* in English came through Sogyal Rinpoche's *The Tibetan Book of Living and Dying* (1992), Chögyam Trungpa's earlier translations of the Nyingma corpus, and more recently through Sam Harris's *Waking Up* book and app, which is heavily inflected by his training under Tulku Urgyen Rinpoche and Mingyur Rinpoche. Harris characterizes ordinary mindfulness as dualistic and then introduces *rigpa* as "non-dual awareness," "completely unconstructed, unimprovable" — what John Dunne has called "non-dual mindfulness" (Dunne, *Contemporary Buddhism* 12:1, 2011).

Two confusions multiply here. First, *rigpa* is a tradition-specific term embedded in a transmission lineage; its recognition (*ngo sprod*, "introduction") is normatively given by a qualified lama, not derived by inference from a phenomenology of attention. Second, when "awareness" in Theravāda or MBSR contexts is silently colored by the connotations of *rigpa* — primordial, always-already-perfect, beyond effort — practitioners are presented with a hybrid object that exists in no actual contemplative tradition.

### 1.8 *Appamāda* (不放逸)

*Appamāda* (Sanskrit *apramāda*; Chinese/Japanese 不放逸 *fuhōitsu*) means "heedfulness" or "non-negligence." It is the Buddha's last word in the *Mahāparinibbāna Sutta*: *appamādena sampādetha* ("strive with heedfulness"). The *Visuddhimagga* and the *Atthasālinī* gloss it as *sati* not slackening — a sustained vigilance over wholesome states, the absence of carelessness with respect to the path. The *Dhammapada* devotes its second chapter to *appamāda* and equates its loss with death.

In English, *appamāda* often gets rendered as "mindful awareness," "diligent awareness," or simply "mindfulness," which loses the moral-volitional charge entirely. *Appamāda* is closer to "moral wakefulness" than to any neutral cognitive state. Bhikkhu Bodhi has repeatedly emphasized that translating *appamāda* as "awareness" smuggles out the central ethical urgency of the Buddha's exhortation.

### 1.9 *Jāgariyā*

*Jāgariyā* — "wakefulness," from the root *jāgṛ-* "to be awake" — appears in the *Jāgariyā Sutta* (AN 4.37) and elsewhere as one of the qualities of the bhikkhu who is "yoked to wakefulness" (*jāgariyaṃ anuyutto*), staying alert through the watches of the night, not sleeping while defilements grow. It overlaps semantically with *appamāda* but emphasizes literal and metaphorical wakefulness rather than non-negligence. Modern English translators sometimes render it "awareness" or "alertness"; the loss is the imagery of staying physically awake, and the ascetic discipline implied.

### 1.10 *Āloka* (明)

*Āloka* ("light," "illumination"; Chinese/Japanese 明 *myō*) is a recurring metaphor in Pāli for cognitive clarity: *ālokasaññā* (perception of light) is one of the meditative perceptions cultivated to dispel sloth and torpor (AN 7.61, AN 6.31). *Vijjā* (knowledge, Sanskrit *vidyā*) shares the root, and is opposed to *avijjā* (ignorance). In Mahāyāna and Vajrayāna texts, *prabhāsvara-citta* (clear-light mind) carries similar luminosity-metaphors. The English word "awareness," when used to evoke a "luminous" quality, draws unacknowledged on this strand — but the specific technical sense of *āloka-saññā* (a deliberately cultivated counter to drowsiness, marked by the perception of brightness) is rarely recovered.

## PART 2 — The Western Genealogy of "Awareness" in Spiritual and Psychological Contexts

### 2a. The Theosophical and Orientalist layer (1880s–1950s)

The English word "mindfulness" enters Buddhist studies in 1881, when T. W. Rhys Davids, founder of the Pali Text Society, uses it for *sati* in his translation of the *Mahāparinibbāna Sutta* in the *Sacred Books of the East*. As Gethin notes, the dictionaries available to Rhys Davids (Monier-Williams 1872, Childers 1875, Böhtlingk and Roth 1855–1875) all foregrounded the *memory* sense of *smṛti*: "remembrance, memory, reminiscence, recollection." Rhys Davids departed from this consensus, choosing instead an archaic English word that suggested attentive presence. He never explained the choice in print. The decision shaped a century of subsequent translation: by 1910 "mindfulness" had become standard in English-language Theravāda materials.

This layer was entangled with the Theosophical Society (founded 1875), whose universalist comparative-religion frame placed Buddhist "consciousness," Hindu *cit*, and Western "spirit" into a single ahistorical category. Olcott's *Buddhist Catechism* (1881) and Theosophy-influenced Buddhist revivalism in Sri Lanka contributed to a vocabulary in which Buddhist technical terms became names for putatively universal mystical experiences — exactly the "perennialist" frame that Robert Sharf later critiqued.

D. T. Suzuki's massive influence on English-language Zen (essays beginning 1927, *Zen Buddhism and Psychoanalysis* with Fromm 1960) introduced "awareness" as the preferred term for Zen *satori*-related cognitive shifts, often deliberately distinguishing Zen "awareness" from Theravāda "mindfulness" as more spontaneous, non-dual, and aesthetic. Suzuki's vocabulary fed directly into the American counterculture via Alan Watts and Gary Snyder.

The most consequential single text in the immediate postwar period was Nyanaponika Thera's *The Heart of Buddhist Meditation* (Buddhist Publication Society, 1962). The German-born monk (Siegmund Feniger, 1901–1994), co-founder of the BPS, translated *sati* in the *Satipaṭṭhāna Sutta* via the gloss "bare attention" — defined as "the clear and single-minded awareness of what actually happens to us and in us, at the successive moments of perception." This phrase, "bare attention," became the dominant interpretive lens through which the next two generations — Joseph Goldstein, Sharon Salzberg, Jack Kornfield, and Jon Kabat-Zinn — understood *sati*. Nyanaponika's *The Power of Mindfulness* (1968) extended the formulation, listing four "powers" of bare attention: the capacity to "name" experience with dispassion; non-coercive receptivity; the slowing of the mind; and seeing things directly.

But, as Bhikkhu Bodhi repeatedly emphasized in his published correspondence with B. Alan Wallace and in his Wisdom Publications introductions, Nyanaponika himself regarded "bare attention" as *only the first phase* of right mindfulness — the entry portal, not the whole house. The fuller practice required integration with *sampajañña*. The popularization of the slogan disconnected it from the qualifier. What Robert Sharf has called "the bare attention thesis" — the equation of *satipaṭṭhāna* with a non-judgmental, non-discursive attending to moment-to-moment experience — became the standard Western reading by the 1980s.

### 2b. The counterculture layer (1960s–1970s)

By the time Richard Alpert returned from India as Ram Dass and published *Be Here Now* (Lama Foundation, 1971), the technical Buddhist vocabulary was already being absorbed into a broader counterculture lexicon. Alpert's own initiation was through Timothy Leary's Harvard psilocybin experiments (the famous snowy Boston night in 1961, recounted in *Be Here Now*), in which the experience was described as "pure awareness 'minding the store,'" stripped of social and physical identity. The route from there to *Be Here Now* — via Bhagavan Das in Nepal and Neem Karoli Baba — fused Hindu *advaita*, Theravāda *vipassanā* (Alpert had also sat with S. N. Goenka), Tibetan teachings, and psychedelic phenomenology under a single rubric: *awareness*. Ram Dass's later coinage "loving awareness" captures the move precisely — *awareness* is no longer a cognitive faculty; it has become an affective state, a metaphysical ground, and a moral disposition all at once.

Alan Watts, building on Suzuki and on his own readings of Daoism, used "awareness" to gloss everything from *wú-wéi* to Zen *satori* to the Vedantic *sākṣin* ("witness"). Watts's gift was popularization at the cost of precision: the lectures collected in *The Way of Zen* (1957), *The Book* (1966), and the posthumous compilations dissolve distinctions between *vijñāna*, *prajñā*, *cit*, *rigpa*, and "consciousness" in service of a unified poetic vision.

Chögyam Trungpa (1939–1987), the most influential Tibetan teacher of the early Western Vajrayāna scene, brought a different lexicon: "awareness" as translation for *shes rab* (*prajñā*), *rig pa*, and various Mahāmudrā/Dzogchen terms in his *Cutting Through Spiritual Materialism* (1973) and *Shambhala: The Sacred Path of the Warrior* (1984). His phrase "panoramic awareness" gestured toward *rigpa*-like non-dual recognition while remaining accessible to readers unfamiliar with Tibetan technical literature — and in the process placed Theravāda and Dzogchen vocabulary on a single English continuum.

Timothy Leary's psychedelic vocabulary — "expanded awareness," "consciousness expansion," the "Eight-Circuit Model of Consciousness" — added a further layer. Awareness became a quantitative variable: one could have "more" or "less" of it, "expand" it, "alter" it. This metric framing flowed seamlessly into Esalen and the Human Potential Movement.

By the late 1970s, "awareness" had come to function in countercultural usage as a portmanteau covering: Buddhist *sati*, Hindu *sākṣin*, Dzogchen *rigpa*, Zen *kenshō*, psychedelic phenomenology, Gestalt "contact," Jungian individuation, and a generic ethic of "being present." The technical sense of any one of these had been almost entirely worn smooth.

### 2c. The Human Potential Movement and self-help (1970s–1990s)

The Esalen Institute (founded 1962 in Big Sur by Michael Murphy and Richard Price) was the institutional engine of this synthesis. From 1964 until his death in 1970, Fritz Perls ran Gestalt Therapy workshops there. Perls's slogan — "awareness per se, by and of itself, is curative" — placed *awareness* at the experiential center of Gestalt practice. In Gestalt's technical lexicon (developed with Paul Goodman and Ralph Hefferline in *Gestalt Therapy*, 1951), awareness meant something more specific than its later popularizations suggested: the figure-ground organization of present experience, including bodily sensation, emotion, and contact with the environment. Perls in *Gestalt Therapy Verbatim* (1969, drawn from Esalen workshops) located growth in the moment-by-moment recovery of disowned material from peripheral awareness into focal awareness — a process closer to phenomenology and existential analysis than to Buddhist *sati*.

But Perls himself worried about how his work was being received. In his final theoretical statement he warned against the "peddlers of instant Joy, the turner-onners of the so-called easy road of sensory liberation" — an explicit rebuke to the Esalen-style theatricalization of awareness as instant breakthrough. The warning largely went unheeded.

Werner Erhard's est (Erhard Seminars Training, founded 1971, succeeded by The Forum and Landmark Education) industrialized the model. The two-weekend est intensive promised "transformation" through a series of high-intensity processes whose currency was *awareness* — specifically, awareness of how one's self-concept and habitual narrative ("the story") constructed one's experience. Erhard borrowed eclectically from Zen (he had trained briefly under Zen Master Yasutani-roshi and read widely in Suzuki), Scientology, Dale Carnegie, Mind Dynamics, and Gestalt; the resulting compound presented "awareness" as a *transformative cognitive event* that could be reliably engineered in a hotel ballroom. Through Landmark and its many imitators (and through est-influenced personalities such as Tony Robbins, who took the structure into mass-market motivational speaking), "awareness" entered the bloodstream of American corporate training and self-help. Perls's own student Richard Bandler, together with John Grinder, codified Gestalt-style intervention into Neuro-Linguistic Programming (NLP), which then exported "awareness work" globally as a packaged technique.

Tony Robbins, Brian Tracy, Stephen Covey, and the broader self-improvement industry of the 1980s–1990s redefined awareness yet again — as *self-knowledge*: knowing one's values, one's limiting beliefs, one's "why." Here the word's meaning has drifted entirely free of any contemplative reference. It has become roughly equivalent to "metacognition about one's own life goals." The continuity with *sati* exists only at the level of an English-language family resemblance.

### 2d. The clinical mindfulness layer (1979–present)

In 1979 Jon Kabat-Zinn, a molecular biologist with Zen training (he had attended retreats with Philip Kapleau, Seung Sahn, and Thich Nhat Hanh), founded the Stress Reduction Clinic at the University of Massachusetts Medical School. The eight-week Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR) protocol he developed there became the template for two generations of clinical interventions: MBCT (Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy for depression, Segal/Williams/Teasdale 2002), MBRP (Relapse Prevention), MB-EAT (Eating Awareness Training), and others.

Kabat-Zinn's operational definition has been quoted, modified, and re-quoted thousands of times. The canonical 1994 formulation: "Mindfulness means paying attention in a particular way: on purpose, in the present moment, and nonjudgmentally" (*Wherever You Go, There You Are*, pp. 3–4). A slightly later, equally famous version: "The awareness that arises through paying attention, on purpose, in the present moment, and nonjudgmentally to the unfolding of experience moment by moment" (*Clinical Psychology: Science and Practice*, 2003). In interviews he has glossed "nonjudgmental" as not absence of preferences but "becoming aware of how judgmental you are, and then not judging that." A still more recent formulation he sometimes uses adds: "in the service of self-understanding and wisdom" — a later qualifier that softens the apparent ethical neutrality of the earlier versions.

What this definition does, from a Buddhist-technical standpoint, is to collapse at least four distinct categories into one operational construct:
- *manasikāra* ("paying attention," the universal directing factor),
- *yoniso manasikāra* ("in a particular way... on purpose," the wise mode of attention),
- *sati* ("the awareness that arises," a wholesome trainable factor), and
- *sampajañña* ("nonjudgmentally to the unfolding of experience," the metacognitive function of clear comprehension).

The collapse is not accidental. Gethin shows that Kabat-Zinn was working under Nyanaponika's "bare attention" reading, supplemented by Zen and Krishnamurti, and his explicit project was to produce a single operational definition that could be measured, taught, and tested. The cost was the loss of every distinction the Abhidhamma had been at pains to draw. As Bhikkhu Bodhi, Paul Grossman, Nicholas Van Dam, and others have argued (Grossman & Van Dam, "Mindfulness, by Any Other Name," *Contemporary Buddhism* 12:1, 2011), the resulting construct does not match any one term in any Buddhist tradition; it is a *new English-language hybrid* held together by the elasticity of the words "attention" and "awareness."

Operationalization for psychometric measurement (the Mindful Attention Awareness Scale, the Five Facet Mindfulness Questionnaire, the Toronto Mindfulness Scale) further fragmented the construct: the FFMQ alone breaks "mindfulness" into observing, describing, acting with awareness, non-judging, and non-reactivity. As Van Dam et al. argued in *Perspectives on Psychological Science* (2018), there is no consensus on what these scales are measuring, and the term *mindfulness* in clinical research now refers to a moving target.

By the early 2010s, the slippage from "mindfulness" to "awareness" was largely complete in popular discourse. Headspace, Calm, and similar apps describe their core offering in terms of "awareness," "presence," "noticing." Kabat-Zinn's careful "paying attention... in the present moment" had become — through hundreds of derivative restatements — simply *awareness*.

### 2e. The contemporary "awareness" industry

Sam Harris's *Waking Up: A Guide to Spirituality Without Religion* (book 2014, app 2018) marks a further turn. Harris, who trained in both Theravāda vipassanā (with Joseph Goldstein, Sharon Salzberg) and Dzogchen (with Tulku Urgyen, Mingyur Rinpoche, and through teachers such as Loch Kelly), introduces "consciousness" or "awareness" as the non-dual ground of experience and explicitly positions Dzogchen-style "pointing-out" instructions as the methodologically superior approach. The Waking Up app distinguishes "dualistic" mindfulness (subject-attending-to-object) from "non-dual" awareness (the self-cognizing nature of mind itself) — a distinction borrowed almost verbatim from Tibetan Mahāmudrā/Dzogchen texts but presented to a secular audience who have no exposure to the underlying tradition. In a 2017 conversation with Joseph Goldstein, Harris explicitly argued for the superiority of Dzogchen over vipassanā on phenomenological grounds — a striking move that places a tradition-specific lineage transmission inside a content-free "spirituality" frame.

The same period saw the rise of: Eckhart Tolle's *The Power of Now* (1997) and *A New Earth* (2005), in which "presence" or "awareness" is identified with the unconditioned ground beneath thought; the boom in "consciousness studies" as a popular genre (Annaka Harris's *Conscious* 2019, the IDW podcast circuit); the corporate adoption of mindfulness at Google (Chade-Meng Tan's "Search Inside Yourself"), Goldman Sachs, Aetna, the U.S. Marine Corps' Mindfulness-Based Mind Fitness Training; and the expansion of the wellness app market into a multi-billion-dollar industry whose central marketed product is some version of "awareness."

In Ronald Purser's analysis (*McMindfulness: How Mindfulness Became the New Capitalist Spirituality*, Repeater Books, 2019), what emerged is a "neoliberal spirituality" in which awareness becomes a privatized cognitive technology for adjusting individuals to systemic stressors — *self*-awareness as a path to *self*-management as a path to *productivity*. The structural critique echoes earlier critiques of est: a Buddhist technical term, once embedded in monastic ethics, ascetic discipline, and a soteriological aim of liberation, has been refunctioned as a tool of accommodation to the conditions that produce suffering rather than a path beyond them.

## PART 3 — Japanese Context: 気づき (*kizuki*)

### 3.1 The ordinary-language meaning

気づき is the nominalization of the verb 気づく (*kizuku*), "to notice, to become aware, to realize." Its semantic field in ordinary Japanese is broad: noticing that someone has cut their hair, realizing one has forgotten one's wallet, suddenly understanding the point of a story. The compound 気 (*ki*) — vital energy, attention, mind-feeling — gives the word a connotation closer to "something coming into one's *ki*" than the English "awareness" suggests. Until the late twentieth century, 気づき was rarely used as a technical term; it belonged to the register of everyday colloquial Japanese, similar to English "noticing" or "realizing."

### 3.2 The rise of 気づき as the standard translation of "mindfulness"

The decisive shift came through two channels. The first was the translation of Thich Nhat Hanh's works. Shunjusha's edition of *The Miracle of Mindfulness* (in Japanese 『〈気づき〉の奇跡』, translated by Ikeda Hisayo), and Shinsensha's 『ブッダの〈気づき〉の瞑想』(*The Buddha's Meditation on Mindfulness*, translated by Shimada Keisuke and others), enclose 気づき in angle brackets (〈 〉) — a typographic convention signaling that the word is being used as a technical term, not in its ordinary sense. Through this convention, the publishers and translators flagged that 〈気づき〉 was a *neologistic technical translation* of *sati* / mindfulness, distinct from the everyday word. The decision has stuck: nearly every subsequent Japanese title on mindfulness — by Shimada Keisuke, by Kumano Hideki, by Fujita Issho, by the Tokuma and Sangha imprints — uses 気づき as the equivalent.

The second channel was the import of MBSR. Haruki Yutaka's translation of Kabat-Zinn's *Full Catastrophe Living* (北大路書房, as 『マインドフルネスストレス低減法』) helped install the loanword マインドフルネス (*maindofurunesu*) alongside 気づき; in current Japanese clinical usage, the two function as near-synonyms, with マインドフルネス preferred in clinical/academic writing and 気づき preferred in popular Buddhist and self-help contexts.

### 3.3 The tension with more precise Japanese Buddhist terms

Japanese Buddhist Chinese already had a sophisticated technical vocabulary for what is now flattened into 気づき:

- 念 (*nen*) — the standard Sino-Japanese rendering of *sati*/*smṛti*, with the same complex of recollection/memory/keeping-in-mind senses; central in Pure Land (e.g. 念仏 *nenbutsu*) and Zen.
- 正念 (*shōnen*) — *sammā-sati*, "right mindfulness" of the eightfold path; in Zen also "correct attention to one's koan."
- 正知 (*shōchi*) — *sampajañña*/*samprajanya*, clear comprehension.
- 作意 (*sai*/*sa-i*) — *manasikāra*, attention/adverting.
- 覚 (*kaku*) — awakening, in *buddha* 仏陀 (the awakened one), 覚者 (the awakened one), 覚知 (*kakuchi*, awareness/cognizing).
- 識 (*shiki*) — *viññāṇa*/*vijñāna*, consciousness.
- 慧 (*e* / *chi-e*) — *paññā*/*prajñā*, wisdom.
- 不放逸 (*fuhōitsu*) — *appamāda*, heedfulness.

Each of these is a settled technical term in Japanese Buddhist scholarship and in sectarian usage. The substitution of 気づき for "mindfulness" obscures all of them. In particular, the loss of 念 is striking: Japanese has had a perfectly good rendering of *sati* for over a millennium, and the choice to substitute 気づき is essentially a marketing decision — 念 carries Buddhist sectarian weight (especially Pure Land associations through 念仏) and is felt as "religious," while 気づき is felt as "experiential," "psychological," "modern." The well-being literature (e.g. articles in the Well-Being Design society) generally explains: "サティ (sati) is translated in Chinese as 念, and its meanings include not only 気づき (noticing) but also memory, recollection, and remembrance" — registering the loss of the memory dimension that Gethin and Bodhi flagged in English, but routinely sliding into 気づき as the operational term anyway.

### 3.4 The influence of Thich Nhat Hanh

Thich Nhat Hanh's Japanese reception, mediated primarily by Shimada Keisuke (the principal translator and proprietor of the workshop space ゆとり家 *yutori-ya*), has been the single greatest force in establishing 気づき as the dominant Japanese term. Shimada's translations — 『ブッダの〈気づき〉の瞑想』, 『ブッダの〈呼吸〉の瞑想』, 『ブッダの〈今を生きる〉瞑想』, 『リトリート』, 『大地に触れる瞑想』 — adopt 気づき consistently for what Thich Nhat Hanh in English calls "mindfulness." Because Thich Nhat Hanh's English itself was already a simplified, lyrical idiom designed for non-specialist audiences, the Japanese 気づき absorbed all the simplifications already built into the English source. Shimada's own work (e.g. 『奇跡をひらくマインドフルネスの旅』, Sangha-Shinsha) explicitly identifies 気づき as the practice's center.

### 3.5 The Japanese self-help (自己啓発) industry and coaching/NLP

By the 2000s, 気づき had become a keyword in the Japanese 自己啓発 (*jiko-keihatsu*, self-development) industry — books, seminars, coaching certifications. The Japanese coaching industry, heavily influenced by Anthony Robbins, the International Coach Federation, and Neuro-Linguistic Programming (NLP imported in the 1990s by Japanese trainers in the Bandler/Grinder lineage), made 気づき the central goal of the coaching conversation. The standard claim — "coaching is the technology of provoking 気づき in the client" (コーチングはクライアントに気づきを促す技術) — collapses *sati*, *sampajañña*, Gestalt awareness, Erhardian "transformation," and ordinary self-reflection into one Japanese term.

In Japanese corporate training (*kenshū*), 気づき has become a checklist outcome: trainees are asked to write 気づきシート (awareness sheets) after sessions, and managers evaluate whether sufficient 気づき has been "obtained." This bureaucratic operationalization has further hollowed the term. In Japanese popular psychology and TV-style life-coaching, 気づき functions as a virtuous noun that legitimizes almost any introspective intervention.

### 3.6 What is specifically Japanese about the confusion

Two features distinguish the Japanese 気づき trajectory from the English "awareness" trajectory:

First, in Japanese the confusion is partially *masked* by the availability of more precise terms (念, 正念, 正知, etc.), which native readers *could* recognize as the "real" technical equivalents — yet which are systematically *not used* in the mindfulness and self-help literature. The reader is often unaware that a less ambiguous vocabulary exists right next door. The angle-bracket convention 〈気づき〉 signals technicality but provides no doctrinal anchoring.

Second, 気 itself carries connotations from East Asian medicine and martial arts (*qi*/*ki* as vital energy) that the English word "awareness" does not. 気づき therefore subtly imports an energy-metaphysics into a discourse that, in English, remains nominally psychological. A Japanese coaching client who is asked to "have 気づき" is being invited into a slightly different conceptual space than an English coaching client asked to "become aware" — the Japanese space is more somatic, more energetic, more diffusely metaphysical. This makes 気づき both more appealing and more semantically slippery than its English counterpart.

## PART 4 — A Typology of the Confusions

The polysemy of "awareness" / 気づき is not a harmless ambiguity. It generates systematic, repeatable confusions that distort meditation instruction, clinical research, philosophical analysis, and contemplative pedagogy. The following ten confusions are the most damaging.

**1. The attention–consciousness conflation.** *Manasikāra* (the universal mental factor directing mind to object) and *viññāṇa* (bare cognizing) are treated as the same thing as "awareness." But the former is a *function* and the latter is a *category of mental events*. Practitioners are told to "be aware" without being told whether to do something (direct attention) or to notice something (consciousness already cognizing). The result is a kind of paralysis or, more often, an unanalyzed muddle.

**2. The mindfulness–awareness conflation.** *Sati*, a wholesome, trainable, ethically charged mental factor with the specific function of non-floating recollection, is identified with "awareness" as a general background quality. The trainable factor disappears into a putative ground state, and the practitioner who is told to "rest in awareness" no longer knows whether they are being asked to cultivate something or to recognize what is already there.

**3. The concept–nonconcept conflation.** "Awareness" is used to translate both ordinary conceptual recognition (*saññā*, *manasikāra*) and putatively non-conceptual cognition (*rigpa*, *prabhāsvara-citta*). When a contemporary teacher tells students to drop into "pure awareness," they may be importing Dzogchen non-dual recognition into a context where it has no doctrinal basis and no transmission framework, while their students hear an instruction to attend to their breath. This is precisely the binary that creates so much confusion in contemporary mindfulness teaching: the conceptual/non-conceptual distinction is real in some traditions, vacuous in others, and the word "awareness" gives the practitioner no way to tell which they are being offered.

**4. The active–passive conflation.** *Sati* is active and effortful — it must be aroused, sustained, and "established" (the meaning of *upaṭṭhāna* in *satipaṭṭhāna*). *Rigpa* is described as effortless, unfabricated, already-perfect. "Awareness" in English equivocates between the two, sometimes within the same sentence ("just rest in effortless awareness — but bring it back when you notice you've wandered"). The practitioner is asked simultaneously to *do* something and to *not do* anything.

**5. The ethical–neutral conflation.** *Sammā-sati* — right mindfulness — is intrinsically ethically directed (the four *satipaṭṭhānas* are oriented toward the seeing of impermanence, dissatisfaction, non-self, and the abandonment of craving). Stripping the *sammā* (right) from *sati* and translating only *sati* as "awareness" produces a content-neutral cognitive technology that can be applied equally to compassion meditation, sniper training, or selling life insurance. The U.S. Marine Corps' Mindfulness-Based Mind Fitness Training and Aetna's corporate wellness programs both depend on this neutralization. Bhikkhu Bodhi and Purser have both argued that this is not a side effect but a constitutive feature of the way "awareness" has been re-engineered for secular markets.

**6. The mundane–supramundane conflation.** Pāli Buddhism distinguishes mundane (*lokiya*) and supramundane (*lokuttara*) mental factors; *sati* operates at both levels but with different objects and effects. "Awareness," with no such marker, is applied indifferently to noticing one's coffee, recognizing one's emotional state, and gnostically apprehending the unconditioned. The categorical jump from one to the other is hidden by the shared word.

**7. The Buddhist–therapeutic conflation.** MBSR's deliberate secularization required removing Buddhist soteriological aims (the cessation of *taṇhā*, the realization of *nibbāna*) while retaining Buddhist methods. The resulting product is sold as if it were Buddhist (the lineage is regularly invoked for credibility) and as if it were not (the goal is symptom reduction, not awakening). "Awareness" is the lexical hinge on which this double game turns.

**8. The individual–social conflation.** In English, "awareness" denotes both personal phenomenal consciousness ("self-awareness") and social-political consciousness ("raising awareness of racism," "awareness campaign," "Breast Cancer Awareness Month"). The two senses are unrelated semantically — they share an English word, not a concept — but the slide between them allows individual contemplative practice to be marketed as a form of social engagement, and social-justice work to borrow the affective valence of meditation.

**9. The Dzogchen–Theravāda conflation.** Perhaps the most consequential confusion in contemporary teaching. *Rigpa* is a Dzogchen-specific term embedded in transmission lineage, in pointing-out instruction, in the view that mind's nature is already pure and unfabricated. Theravāda *sati* is a cultivated factor on a path of gradual purification, with no doctrinal claim that mind's nature is already pure. When non-dual *rigpa* language is imported into Theravāda or MBSR contexts under the umbrella of "awareness" (as in Sam Harris's app, or in the various "non-dual mindfulness" movements analyzed by John Dunne), practitioners are given a hybrid object that neither tradition endorses.

**10. The phenomenological–ontological conflation.** "Awareness" is sometimes used to name a quality of experience (something one *has*) and sometimes to name a metaphysical fundamental (something one *is*, or that *is the ground* of reality). Contemporary "consciousness fundamentalism" (panpsychist popularizers, Bernardo Kastrup, Annaka Harris, Federico Faggin) trades on this slide: from "awareness is present in this moment of experience" to "Awareness is the fundamental ground of being." Sharf's 1995 critique anticipated exactly this move — the slide from technical terms naming experiential events to substantive nouns naming metaphysical entities.

## PART 5 — Why This Matters

### 5.1 Distortion of meditation instruction

When "awareness" stands in for ten technically distinct Buddhist terms, the practitioner has no way to tell which instruction they are receiving. A teacher who says "rest in awareness" might be invoking *sati*, *rigpa*, *prabhāsvara-citta*, *cit*, *sākṣin*, *manasikāra*, or simply Perlsian phenomenological contact — and the appropriate response differs in each case. The result is widespread practitioner confusion: people who have been told to "be aware" for years, who experience that "awareness" as something effortful and tiring, who then read about "natural awareness" and assume they have been doing it wrong, who then attempt to "drop into" a non-dual recognition they have no preparation for, and who oscillate between these poles indefinitely.

This is the source of the "concept vs. non-concept" binary that haunts contemporary contemplative teaching. The binary is itself an artifact of the lexical collapse: when "awareness" is the single word in English for both the conceptually-mediated wholesome factor and the putatively non-conceptual ground, practitioners cannot help but feel caught between them — because they have not been given any other vocabulary. The binary feels like a deep doctrinal disagreement; in fact it is, in large part, a translation artifact.

### 5.2 Reinforcement of Buddhist modernist distortions

Robert Sharf's 1995 article "Buddhist Modernism and the Rhetoric of Meditative Experience" (*Numen* 42:3, 228–283) argued that the modern equation of Buddhism with private "experience" — with the cultivation of specific phenomenal states — is a colonial and post-colonial reframing of a tradition that historically was about discipline, textual mastery, and ritual practice rather than about *states of consciousness experienced by individual meditators*. Sharf's later "Is Mindfulness Buddhist? (And Why It Matters)" (*Transcultural Psychiatry* 2015) and "Mindfulness and Mindlessness in Early Chan" (*Philosophy East & West* 2014) extended the critique: the "bare attention" reading, with its lineage in Burmese reform movements of the early twentieth century, has obscured a more variegated traditional landscape. Sharf notes that the cultivation of present-centered awareness was not without precedent — similar innovations appeared in medieval Chinese Chan and in Tibetan Dzogchen — but in each case it arose as a reform movement, often as an attempt to make practice accessible to laypersons, and was contested within the tradition.

David McMahan's *The Making of Buddhist Modernism* (Oxford, 2008) showed how Romanticism, Protestant Christianity, scientific naturalism, and Theosophy together produced the modernist Buddhism in which interior experience is the proper object of religious life. Erik Braun's *The Birth of Insight: Meditation, Modern Buddhism, and the Burmese Monk Ledi Sayadaw* (Chicago, 2013) traced the specific lineage: Ledi Sayadaw (1846–1923) in colonial Burma standardized and simplified meditation methods (rooted in *Abhidhamma*) to make them accessible to lay populations under colonial threat; Mahasi Sayadaw and U Ba Khin extended this in the mid-twentieth century; S. N. Goenka, Jack Kornfield, Joseph Goldstein, Sharon Salzberg, and Jon Kabat-Zinn transmitted it to the West. The "awareness" vocabulary is the rhetorical signature of this lineage: it speaks to an audience that wants direct experiential access without doctrinal apparatus, and provides a single English word that can stand in for the whole tradition.

When the polysemy of "awareness" is not flagged, the distinctively modernist reading is presented as if it were Buddhism *tout court*. The historical and doctrinal alternatives — *sati* as recollective memory tied to ethics, *sampajañña* as evaluative metacognition, *paññā* as discernment, *appamāda* as moral wakefulness — disappear from view.

### 5.3 Enabling commercialization

Ronald Purser's *McMindfulness: How Mindfulness Became the New Capitalist Spirituality* (Repeater Books, 2019), expanding on his earlier essay "Beyond McMindfulness," argues that the secularization of mindfulness — and its further rebranding as "awareness" — has produced a privatized cognitive technology for adjusting workers to the conditions of stress that capitalism itself produces. Without a moral compass or ethical commitments, unmoored from a vision of the social good, mindfulness becomes anchored in the ethos of the market. Jeff Wilson's *Mindful America: The Mutual Transformation of Buddhist Meditation and American Culture* (Oxford, 2014) documents the parallel commodification: mindfulness sold as a product line in lifestyle markets, with "awareness" as the abstract good being marketed across coloring books, cookbooks, parenting manuals, and corporate retreats.

The semantic inflation of "awareness" is not incidental to this commercialization; it is *required* by it. A precise technical term embedded in monastic ethics and a specific doctrinal framework cannot be sold to general consumers. A vague, positively-valenced English word that can mean "noticing your breath," "feeling present," "knowing yourself," and "tapping into the ground of being" can be sold to anyone. The word's vagueness is its market virtue. The same logic applies in Japan to 気づき, where the term's openness is what allows it to migrate from monastic Theravāda contexts (via Thich Nhat Hanh) into corporate 研修 trainings.

### 5.4 What more precise terminology would gain

A more disciplined vocabulary — distinguishing *sati* (recollective mindfulness), *sampajañña* (clear comprehension), *manasikāra* (attention/adverting), *yoniso manasikāra* (wise attention), *viññāṇa* (consciousness), *paññā* (discernment), *rigpa* (Dzogchen non-dual cognition), *appamāda* (heedfulness) — would not solve every problem of contemplative pedagogy, but it would solve several:

- It would let practitioners and teachers locate themselves doctrinally: which tradition are we in, which factor are we cultivating, what is the relation between this factor and others?
- It would defuse the active-passive paradox: *sati* is to be cultivated; *rigpa* is to be recognized; these are not the same instruction.
- It would restore the ethical dimension: *sammā-sati* is not content-neutral, and the distinction between *sati* and a generic "awareness" is precisely the difference between a factor with directionality and a factor without.
- It would block the slide from phenomenological description to metaphysical assertion: that "awareness is present" in a moment of experience does not entail that "Awareness is the ground of being," and a more careful vocabulary would force the difference into view.
- It would interrupt the marketing pipeline: a precise term resists commodification in a way that an inflated one does not.

In Japanese, the corresponding move would be to recover 念, 正念, 正知, 作意, and the rest from their relative obsolescence, and to treat 気づき as one (limited, modern, popular) translation rather than as a universal equivalent. The bracketing convention 〈気づき〉 used by Shinsensha and Shunjusha at least signals that the word is doing technical work; but a bracket is not a definition, and the underlying confusions remain.

## Conclusion

"Awareness" and 気づき are not bad words. They are, in their ordinary-language uses, perfectly serviceable. The problem is that they have been recruited to do the work of ten or twelve technically distinct terms in three different Buddhist traditions, two strands of Western therapy, and a now-global wellness industry — and they cannot bear the load.

The historical genealogy traces a clean arc. Rhys Davids in 1881 chooses "mindfulness" over "memory" for *sati*, departing from his dictionaries without explanation. The Theosophical Society and D. T. Suzuki recast Buddhist technical terms as names for universal mystical experiences. Nyanaponika in 1962 fixes the modern meaning further as "bare attention" — even though Nyanaponika himself, as Bhikkhu Bodhi has stressed, never intended this to be the whole of *satipaṭṭhāna*. Ram Dass, Watts, and Trungpa generalize "awareness" as the counterculture's central spiritual word, fusing Hindu, Theravāda, Tibetan, Zen, and psychedelic phenomenology under one lexical roof. Perls and Erhard install it as a therapeutic and transformational currency, with Esalen, est, Landmark, and NLP carrying it into the mainstream. Kabat-Zinn in 1979 packages it as a clinical operational definition that quietly collapses *manasikāra*, *yoniso manasikāra*, *sati*, and *sampajañña* into a single phrase. The apps, the wellness industry, and Sam Harris's *rigpa*-inflected "consciousness" complete the trajectory. In Japan, Thich Nhat Hanh's translators, the Kabat-Zinn import, and the 自己啓発 / coaching / NLP industries together install 気づき as the local equivalent of this fully inflated English term.

The typology of confusions is the price we pay. Practitioners are caught between activity and passivity, between concept and non-concept, between Theravāda *sati* and Dzogchen *rigpa*, between phenomenology and metaphysics, between individual interiority and social activism, without being told that these are different things. Buddhist studies, contemplative pedagogy, clinical research, and ordinary practice all suffer in proportion as the underlying distinctions are erased.

What is wanted is not the abolition of the words "awareness" and 気づき but their *demotion* from universal equivalents to one (limited, modern) translation among others. Where the technical context demands it, the technical term should be used: *sati*, *sampajañña*, *manasikāra*, *yoniso manasikāra*, *viññāṇa*, *paññā*, *rigpa*, *appamāda*. Where the popular context permits it, "awareness" can do its ordinary-language work — but should not be allowed to masquerade as the name of a thing.

The discipline of using the right word for the right concept is itself a form of *sampajañña*: clear comprehension of what we are doing and why. It would be ironic — and apt — if recovering that discipline in our own language turned out to be the most important thing we could learn from the tradition the word "awareness" has been used, for a century and a half, to obscure.
