# From Stimulus to Conscious Experience: A Comparative Study of Three Abhidharma Models of Cognition

## 1. Introduction: Three Solutions to One Problem

The three classical Abhidharma traditions — Theravāda, Sarvāstivāda-Vaibhāṣika, and Yogācāra-Vijñānavāda — share a single *explanandum*: how does a momentary, impersonal stream of *dharmas* produce the experienced unity of a perceptual event? Each tradition agrees that there is no enduring self (*anātman/anatta*), that consciousness is intentional ("always consciousness of"), and that mental events are radically momentary (*kṣaṇikatva / khaṇikavāda*). But they differ profoundly in *how* they bridge the gap between sensory stimulation and the felt, judged, karmically efficacious moment of conscious cognition.

Three idioms emerge:

- **Theravāda** answers *processually* (横の図式): a fixed serial sequence of seventeen discrete consciousness-moments (*citta-vīthi*, 心路過程) interposes itself between an impinging object and the *bhavaṅga* substream.
- **Sarvāstivāda** answers *ontologically* (存在論的): there is no vīthi-like sequence because dharmas of the three times genuinely exist (*sarvāstivāda*, 三世実有・法体恒有); cognition arises through the simultaneous co-presence of *citta* and its concomitants (*caitta*) under a fourfold scheme of conditions (*catvāraḥ pratyayāḥ*, 四縁) and a sixfold scheme of causes (*ṣaḍ hetavaḥ*, 六因).
- **Yogācāra** answers *structurally* (縦の図式): instead of unfolding cognition horizontally in time, it analyses the *internal structure* of a single cognitive moment into four aspects (*catur-bhāga*, 四分) generated by the actualisation (*pravṛtti*, 現行) of seeds (*bīja*, 種子) deposited in the storehouse-consciousness (*ālaya-vijñāna*, 阿頼耶識).

Noa Ronkin's *Early Buddhist Metaphysics* (RoutledgeCurzon 2005) frames this in a useful way: Abhidharma "reduces the time scale" of the Sūtra's psycho-physical processes "so they are now seen as operating from moment to moment," reinterpreting sequential processes as discrete momentary events. The three schools then disagree over which formal resource — temporal serial order, three-time ontology, or vertical structural depth — best does the ensuing analytical work.

## 2. The Theravāda Citta-vīthi: Cognition as a Seventeen-Moment Process

### 2.1 Textual basis

The mature theory is laid out in Buddhaghosa's *Visuddhimagga* (XIV.111–124, XVII.126–145) and *Atthasālinī* (*Dhs-a* 82–106, 267–287), and is given canonical schematic form in Anuruddha's *Abhidhammatthasaṅgaha* (ca. 11th–12th c. CE), Chapter IV. The system rests on (i) a doctrine of strict momentariness in which a single *citta-khaṇa* is exhausted in three sub-moments (*uppāda* arising, *ṭhiti* presence, *bhaṅga* dissolution), and (ii) the temporal correlation that one unit of matter (*rūpa-kalāpa*) lasts seventeen mind-moments.

### 2.2 The seventeen moments for a "very great" visible object at the eye-door (*atimahantārammaṇa pañcadvāra-vīthi*)

| # | Pāli term | Chinese | Function | Module |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | *atīta-bhavaṅga* | 過去有分 | Past life-continuum | Pre-cognitive |
| 2 | *bhavaṅga-calana* | 有分動 | Vibration of bhavaṅga | Pre-cognitive |
| 3 | *bhavaṅga-upaccheda* | 有分断 | Arrest of bhavaṅga | Pre-cognitive |
| 4 | *pañcadvārāvajjana* | 五門転向 | Five-door adverting (attention) | **M1 Adverting** |
| 5 | *cakkhu-viññāṇa* | 眼識 | Eye-consciousness; bare seeing | **M2 Sensing** |
| 6 | *sampaṭicchana* | 領受 | Receiving | **M3 Reception** |
| 7 | *santīraṇa* | 推度 | Investigating | **M3 Reception** |
| 8 | *voṭṭhapana* | 確定 | Determining | **M3 Reception** |
| 9–15 | *javana* × 7 | 速行 | Impulsion; karmically efficacious "running" | **M4 Apperception** |
| 16–17 | *tadārammaṇa* × 2 | 彼所縁 | Registration; "tasting" the residue | **M4 Apperception** |

After moment 17 the stream lapses (*vīthi-bhaṅga*) back into bhavaṅga. The bhavaṅga state (literally "limb of becoming," 有分) is the passive continuum that persists in dreamless sleep, deep coma, and between cognitive episodes — Theravāda's mechanism for solving the **continuity problem**.

Several structural features deserve emphasis. (a) The object is "very great" precisely because three bhavaṅgas have already elapsed by the time mind engages with it — by the time you become conscious of a visible form, much of its lifespan as matter has already passed. Weaker objects (*mahanta*, *paritta*, *atiparitta*) truncate the series at registration, javana, voṭṭhapana, or never enter cognition at all. (b) Eye-consciousness (moment 5) is just bare seeing; conceptual judgement is performed by later moments, not by sense-consciousness itself. (c) Only the seven javana moments generate new karma; reception, investigation, determination, and registration are *vipāka* or *kiriya* and karmically inert. (d) Crucially, the *adverting* moment *precedes* sense-consciousness: attention is not something the conscious mind does — attention is what *makes* sense-consciousness possible.

A separate but parallel series, the *manodvāra-vīthi* (意門路過程), governs purely mental cognition. It omits the first three bhavaṅga moments (only *bhavaṅga-calana* and *upaccheda* are required), the five-door adverting is replaced by *manodvārāvajjana* (意門転向), and no sense-consciousness intervenes; the object passes directly to javana and registration.

### 2.3 Karunadasa and Ganeri on vīthi

Y. Karunadasa (*The Theravāda Abhidhamma*, 2010) stresses that *vīthi* is not the report of an inner observer but a reconstructive model of what *must* happen for an episode of cognition to be both intentional and karmically responsible without a self. He also notes the cardinal Theravāda principle that no *citta* can cognise itself (the rejection of *taññāṇatā*): self-awareness must be reconstructed across moments, never within one.

Jonardon Ganeri, in *Attention, Not Self* (Oxford 2017), reads Buddhaghosa as proposing an "attention-first" architecture of the mind: *āvajjana* (adverting) and the universal mental factor *manasikāra* together discharge the explanatory work normally assigned to a self. The executive functions of mind — orienting, selecting, judging, sustaining — are unbundled and distributed across the seventeen moments. Ganeri argues that this distribution maps remarkably onto contemporary cognitive-scientific findings about pre-conscious processing latency and attentional bottlenecking.

## 3. The Sarvāstivāda Abhidharmakośa: Cognition Without a Vīthi

The brief poses the central question directly: *how does the Sarvāstivāda system process cognition without a vīthi-like sequential model?* The answer turns on three interlocking moves: (a) shifting the analysis from horizontal time to synchronic co-arising, (b) absorbing what vīthi distributes across moments into the typology of mental factors that arise *together*, and (c) using the three-time ontology and the immediately-antecedent condition to underwrite the lawful succession of such complex moments.

### 3.1 Textual basis

The reference works are the *Mahāvibhāṣā* (ca. 2nd c. CE), Vasubandhu's *Abhidharmakośa-kārikā* with auto-commentary *Bhāṣya* (ca. 4th–5th c. CE), and Saṅghabhadra's orthodox rejoinder *Nyāyānusāra*. The Kośa lists 75 dharmas in five categories, of which 46 are *caitta* (心所法), mental factors that arise simultaneously with *citta*.

### 3.2 No vīthi, but the doctrine of simultaneous co-arising (*saṃprayoga*, 相応)

The decisive break with the Theravāda picture is this: the Sarvāstivāda does **not** distribute the work of cognition across a fixed serial sequence of consciousness-moments. Instead, each consciousness-moment is internally complex — *citta* never arises alone but always with a determinate cluster of *caittas*. The classification of mental factors itself does the work that vīthi does in Theravāda.

The 46 *caittas* are grouped into six *bhūmi*:

1. **Mahābhūmika** (大地法) — **ten** universal factors present in *every* citta whatsoever: *vedanā* (受), *saṃjñā* (想), *cetanā* (思), *sparśa* (触), *chanda* (欲), *prajñā* (慧), *smṛti* (念), **manaskāra (作意, attention)**, *adhimokṣa* (勝解), *samādhi* (定).
2. **Kuśala-mahābhūmika** (大善地法) — ten wholesome universals.
3. **Kleśa-mahābhūmika** (大煩悩地法) — six defiled universals.
4. **Akuśala-mahābhūmika** (大不善地法) — two unwholesome universals.
5. **Upakleśa-bhūmika** (小煩悩地法) — ten minor defilements.
6. **Aniyata** (不定法) — eight ethically variable factors (including *vitarka*, *vicāra*, *kaukṛtya*, *middha*, *rāga*, *pratigha*, *māna*, *vicikitsā*).

*Note on terminology*: the research brief refers to "5 universal mental factors (mahābhūmika)." This formulation conflates two systems: in Sarvāstivāda the *mahābhūmika* are **ten**; the count of **five** universals (*pañca-sarvatraga*) belongs to the Yogācāra reorganisation codified in Asaṅga's *Abhidharma-samuccaya* and the *Cheng Weishi Lun*. The systems disagree precisely on whether *chanda*, *prajñā*, *smṛti*, *adhimokṣa*, and *samādhi* are universal (Sarvāstivāda) or only occasional (Yogācāra, which detaches them and re-files them as *viniyata* "particular" or *kuśala* "wholesome" factors).

### 3.3 The four conditions (四縁 *catvāraḥ pratyayāḥ*) and the six causes (六因 *ṣaḍ hetavaḥ*)

Vasubandhu's *Kośa* II.61–62 explains causation through four *pratyayas*:

| Sanskrit (Chinese) | Function |
|---|---|
| *hetu-pratyaya* (因縁) | Causal condition proper; corresponds to five of the six *hetu*. |
| ***samanantara-pratyaya*** (等無間縁) | "Equal-and-immediately-antecedent" condition: the just-ceased mental moment that opens a "place" for and shapes the immediately following moment. |
| *ālambana-pratyaya* (所縁縁) | Object-condition: the intentional object that supports the arising of a consciousness directed at it. |
| *adhipati-pratyaya* (増上縁) | Dominant or contributory condition: any non-obstructing factor. |

The six causes (六因) further articulate the *hetu-pratyaya*:

| Sanskrit (Chinese) | Function |
|---|---|
| *kāraṇa-hetu* (能作因) | Efficient/instrumental cause; everything non-obstructing of an effect counts. |
| *sahabhū-hetu* (倶有因) | Simultaneity cause; reciprocal causes that arise together (e.g., citta and its caittas mutually cause each other to arise as a complex). |
| *saṃprayuktaka-hetu* (相応因) | Congruence cause; mental factors share one *āśraya* (support), *ālambana* (object), *ākāra* (mental aspect), *kāla* (time), and *dravya* (substance) with the citta they accompany. |
| *sabhāga-hetu* (同類因) | Homogeneous cause; like produces like (a moment of wholesome citta produces a future wholesome moment). |
| *sarvatraga-hetu* (遍行因) | Pervasive cause; certain universal defilements cause all subsequent defilements in their realm. |
| *vipāka-hetu* (異熟因) | Maturation cause; wholesome/unwholesome action ripening as karmically neutral *vipāka*. |

The combined work of *sahabhū-hetu*, *saṃprayuktaka-hetu*, and *samanantara-pratyaya* allows Sarvāstivāda to dispense with vīthi. Within a single moment, sahabhū and saṃprayuktaka license the simultaneous, mutually-conditioning co-arising of citta + caittas under perfect alignment of support, object, aspect, time, and substance. *Between* moments, samanantara-pratyaya guarantees that the just-ceased citta lawfully gives way to a next citta that is appropriately responsive to it. There is therefore no need to insert intervening moments labelled "reception," "investigation," "determination," "registration": each function is realised *within* the moment by the appropriate caitta — *vedanā* registers hedonic tone, *prajñā* discriminates, *adhimokṣa* settles upon the object, *cetanā* impels.

### 3.4 Sarvāstivāda doctrine proper (三世実有・法体恒有)

The school's name comes from its signature ontological claim: "He who affirms the existence of the dharmas of the three time periods is held to be a Sarvāstivādin" (*Kośa* V.25cd). Past dharmas have not vanished into nothing; future dharmas are not yet operative but already exist. The transition from future → present → past is differentiated, in the four classical accounts catalogued in the *Mahāvibhāṣā* and reported by Vasubandhu, by change in *bhāva* (Dharmatrāta), *lakṣaṇa* (Ghoṣaka), *avasthā* (Vasumitra — the orthodox Vaibhāṣika view), or *anyathātva* (Buddhadeva).

This ontology does heavy lifting for the cognitive theory:

- It explains **karma across time**: the past action's dharma still exists and can produce its result.
- It explains **memory and prospection**: mind can take a past or future dharma as *ālambana* because such dharmas genuinely exist as objects.
- It explains the **immediately-antecedent condition**: the just-ceased citta can shape the next citta because, having moved into the "past" mode of existence, it has not become non-existent.
- It dissolves the **continuity problem** ontologically rather than processually: there is no need to posit a bhavaṅga or an ālaya, because dharmas never simply fall out of being.

### 3.5 The role of *manaskāra* (作意)

In Sarvāstivāda, *manaskāra* is a **mahābhūmika** — present in *every* moment of citta, not a separate adverting moment that precedes consciousness. Vasubandhu defines it as "that which bends the mind" (*cetasa ābhogaḥ*) toward an object: an internal direction-fixing, not a temporal antecedent. The Sarvāstivāda thus collapses the Theravāda distinction between *pañcadvārāvajjana* (the adverting moment) and the *manasikāra* cetasika; attention is built into the synchronic structure of every citta rather than functioning as a discrete prior moment.

### 3.6 What Sarvāstivāda lacks and how it copes

Sarvāstivāda has **no module-architecture** of the vīthi type. Distinctions among receptive, investigative, and judgemental functions are absorbed into the typology of *caittas* operating within a single moment. What the Theravāda spreads across time, the Sarvāstivāda compresses into the simultaneous *saṃprayoga* of one moment, while the *samanantara-pratyaya* secures the lawful succession of such moments. The trade-off is clear: the model gains elegance and explanatory economy at the price of forfeiting the fine-grained processing-stage distinctions that vīthi makes visible.

## 4. Yogācāra-Vijñānavāda: The Vertical Analysis of a Single Cognitive Moment

### 4.1 Textual basis

Yogācāra develops out of Sarvāstivāda Abhidharma and the Sautrāntika theory of seeds. Key works: the *Saṃdhinirmocana-sūtra*; Asaṅga's *Mahāyāna-saṃgraha* and *Abhidharma-samuccaya*; Vasubandhu's *Triṃśikā* and *Viṃśatikā*; and, definitively for East Asian Yogācāra, Xuanzang's compendium *Cheng Weishi Lun* (成唯識論, ca. 659 CE), which synthesises ten Indian commentaries on Vasubandhu's *Triṃśikā*, favouring Dharmapāla (護法).

### 4.2 The eight consciousnesses (八識)

The six *pravṛtti-vijñānas* (five sense consciousnesses + *mano-vijñāna*) of earlier Abhidharma are supplemented by two:

- **Manas / kliṣṭa-manas** (末那識): a continuously active "afflicted mentation" that takes the ālaya as its object and falsely reifies it as a self (*ātma-graha*). Manas is the seat of the four primary defilements (*ātma-dṛṣṭi* 我見, *ātma-māna* 我慢, *ātma-sneha* 我愛, *ātma-moha* 我癡).
- **Ālaya-vijñāna** (阿頼耶識): the storehouse-consciousness, also called *sarvabījaka-citta* (一切種子心), *mūla-vijñāna* (根本識), and *ādāna-vijñāna* (執持識). It carries the seeds of all past actions and is the mechanism that solves the continuity problem (no break in dreamless sleep, *nirodha-samāpatti* 滅尽定, or rebirth).

Crucially — as William Waldron emphasises in *The Buddhist Unconscious* (RoutledgeCurzon 2003) — the eight consciousnesses are not arranged as a temporal sequence but as **simultaneous** modes of cognitive operation. The *Saṃdhinirmocana* states that the six manifest awarenesses occur "together with" the ālaya. Waldron calls this "simultaneity of all the modes of cognitive awareness" the most decisive departure from earlier Abhidharma models, in which each vijñāna arose only in conjunction with its own sense-base and object. The ālaya is therefore not a deeper layer beneath consciousness but a constantly-active substrate concurrent with all manifest cognition.

### 4.3 Seeds and their actualisation (種子現行)

Every action perfumes (*vāsanā*, 熏習) the ālaya with seeds (*bīja*, 種子). When conditions ripen, a seed actualises (*pravṛtti*, 現行) as a manifest cognition; that manifest cognition in turn perfumes new seeds. The dynamic is a circuit:

> **種子生現行 → 現行熏種子 → 種子生種子**
> (Seeds generate actuality → actuality perfumes seeds → seeds generate seeds.)

This replaces the Sarvāstivāda doctrine of past-and-future dharmas: instead of dharmas existing in the three times, only present actualised dharmas exist, but the past is preserved as seed-potency and the future arises from seed-ripening. The model is closer to a dispositional ontology than to Vaibhāṣika eternalism.

### 4.4 The four aspects of cognition (四分)

In Dharmapāla's account preserved in the *Cheng Weishi Lun*, every act of consciousness has four internal aspects:

| Aspect (Skt./Chin.) | Function |
|---|---|
| *nimitta-bhāga* (相分) | The "image-part": the appearance of an object within consciousness — what shows up as the object-pole. |
| *darśana-bhāga* (見分) | The "seeing-part": the apprehending function, the subject-pole. |
| *svasaṃvitti-bhāga* (自証分) | "Self-witnessing part": the reflexive awareness that *the seeing has seen the seen*. |
| *svasaṃvitti-saṃvitti-bhāga* (証自証分) | "Witness of the self-witness": the further reflexive guarantee that prevents infinite regress. |

The bhāgas are *not* temporal moments but **simultaneous aspects** of one cognitive event. They are an analysis of cognitive structure, not cognitive process. The subject-pole and object-pole are both produced from the same root consciousness (*mūla-vijñāna*); they are *vikalpa* (分別) — discriminative constructions — not a real subject meeting a real object. This is the technical sense of *vijñapti-mātratā* (唯識): not "only mind exists" in a metaphysical idealist sense (which Dan Lusthaus, *Buddhist Phenomenology*, 2002, argues is a misreading), but "all that we encounter is the differentiation of one cognitive field into apparent subject and object" — an indictment of the closured nature of ordinary cognition rather than an idealist ontology.

The earlier Yogācāra positions on the bhāgas were: one-bhāga (Sthiramati: only the *svasaṃvitti*), two-bhāga (Nanda: *darśana* + *nimitta*), three-bhāga (Dignāga: + *svasaṃvitti*), and four-bhāga (Dharmapāla). The *Cheng Weishi Lun* adopts Dharmapāla's four-fold analysis as orthodox.

Dan Arnold (*Brains, Buddhas, and Believing*, Columbia 2012, esp. ch. 5 on the *svasaṃvitti* doctrine) argues that *svasaṃvedana* in Dignāga and Dharmakīrti is best read as a transcendental rather than a phenomenological doctrine: it is the condition of possibility for there being an episode of cognition at all, not an additional inner perception. Robert Sharf ("Is Yogācāra Phenomenology?" *Journal of Indian Philosophy* 2016), examining the same four aspects in the *Cheng Weishi Lun*, concurs that they bear little resemblance to Husserlian noetic-noematic analysis: they are structural-causal posits within a momentariness ontology, not descriptions of lived experience.

### 4.5 The five universal mental factors (五遍行 *pañca-sarvatraga*)

Yogācāra reorganises the inherited list of *caittas* into fifty-one factors in six groups (*Abhidharma-samuccaya* / *Cheng Weishi Lun*). The first group — universal in *every* moment of *every* consciousness, including ālaya and manas — is:

1. *sparśa* (触) — contact
2. *manaskāra* (作意) — attention
3. *vedanā* (受) — feeling
4. *saṃjñā* (想) — perception/conception
5. *cetanā* (思) — volition

This is the crucial point of contact with the comparative scheme. *Manaskāra* in Yogācāra is a *sarvatraga*: it accompanies *every* cognitive event — including the ālaya-vijñāna and the manas. Unlike Theravāda, attention is not a discrete prior moment; unlike Sarvāstivāda, it is even built into the deepest substratum consciousness. The other five Yogācāra categories of mental factor are *viniyata* (particular, 別境, 5 factors), *kuśala* (wholesome, 善, 11), *kleśa* (root affliction, 根本煩悩, 6), *upakleśa* (secondary affliction, 随煩悩, 20), and *aniyata* (indeterminate, 不定, 4).

### 4.6 Direction of cognition: inside-out

Because cognition is the actualisation of internal seeds projected as apparent subject and object, Yogācāra reverses the directional metaphor of vīthi. In Theravāda, cognition flows *outside-in*: stimulus → sensory processing → consciousness. In Yogācāra, cognition flows *inside-out*: ālaya-vijñāna → seed-ripening → manifest consciousness → apparent (constructed) object. The "external" stimulus, on the strongest reading of *vijñapti-mātratā*, is itself a function of the karmically conditioned seed-stock rather than an extra-mental impingement.

|---|---|---|---|

### 5.2 Temporal structure

The three systems exhibit three orthogonal solutions to the puzzle that a momentary stimulus produces a phenomenally extended experience.

- **Theravāda** distributes the work over time: between stimulus and conscious uptake stand 4–5 mind-moments (atīta-bhavaṅga through cakkhu-viññāṇa); between sensation and judgement stand the receptive-investigative-determining moments; between judgement and karmic agency stand the seven javanas. There is therefore a built-in *lag* between stimulation and consciousness — a feature Ganeri (2017) and Karunadasa (2010) both note is uncannily compatible with empirical findings on attentional and pre-conscious processing.
- **Sarvāstivāda** denies the lag *ontologically*: the past stimulus, the present cognition, and the future result are all real; *samanantara-pratyaya* governs the seamless succession of moments without requiring any mediating processing stage. The lawfulness of cognition is encoded in the typology of causal relations, not in a stage-architecture. As Ronkin (2005) and Collett Cox have argued, this is what makes Sarvāstivāda's metaphysics "intrinsic-nature-realist" in a way that exposes it to Sautrāntika and Madhyamaka critique.
- **Yogācāra** internalises the lag: the temporal extension that vīthi spreads over seventeen moments is collapsed into the depth-structure of one moment in which seeds ripen into manifest vijñāna with its four aspects. Past experience is present *as* perfumed potentiality.

### 5.3 The continuity problem

What persists when the cognitive process lapses — in dreamless sleep, the meditative attainment of cessation (*nirodha-samāpatti*), unconsciousness, or between death and rebirth? Each tradition offers a distinct mechanism:

- Theravāda: *bhavaṅga-citta* — a passive, undisturbed, sub-cognitive resultant consciousness that flows on, providing the karmic and existential link.
- Sarvāstivāda: nothing dynamic is needed, because no dharma ever falls out of being. *Nirodha-samāpatti* is itself a *cittaviprayukta-saṃskāra* (a dharma dissociated from thought). Past karmic dharmas remain real and can still produce results.
- Yogācāra: *ālaya-vijñāna*. Strikingly, Yogācāra argues against bhavaṅga *and* against Vaibhāṣika "all-exists" by offering a *third* solution — a substratum consciousness that itself never lapses, carries the seeds, and supports rebirth. Waldron (2003) shows how the ālaya is best understood as the systematic integration of all the continuity-problems earlier Abhidharma left unresolved: *āśraya-parivṛtti* (轉依, "transformation of the basis") at awakening just is the elimination of the seed-store.

### 5.4 The position of attention

| | Adverting / preceding | Co-arising / structural |
|---|---|---|
| Theravāda | *āvajjana* (転向) as moment 4 of vīthi | *manasikāra* as universal cetasika |
| Sarvāstivāda | — | *manaskāra* as *mahābhūmika* (10 universals) |
| Yogācāra | — | *manaskāra* as *sarvatraga* (5 universals), even in ālaya |

Theravāda alone makes attention a temporally prior moment. The two systems with a richer ontology of consciousness make attention a constitutive synchronic feature. This corresponds to the deeper architectural difference: where Theravāda externalises attention as a gating module, Sarvāstivāda and Yogācāra internalise it as a constitutive structural feature. One way to put the disagreement: for Theravāda, attention is what *summons* consciousness; for Sarvāstivāda and Yogācāra, attention is what *every* consciousness already *is*.

It is worth noting that Theravāda also recognises *manasikāra* as one of its seven universal cetasikas. The tradition therefore has *both* — a discrete adverting moment *and* an in-moment attentional factor — whereas the other two have only the latter.

### 5.5 Direction of cognition

The clearest contrast is geometric. Vīthi is centripetal: object impinges, vibrates the substream, is adverted to, seen, received, investigated, determined, and then karmically run-upon by the mind. Yogācāra is centrifugal: seeds in the ālaya ripen, the *darśana-bhāga* projects, the *nimitta-bhāga* appears, and the experienced "object" is the precipitate of an inward process. Sarvāstivāda, lacking either a centripetal vīthi or a centrifugal seed-store, treats the object and the cognition as **mutual conditions** — the object is an *ālambana-pratyaya* of the cognition, the cognition functions via the object, but neither produces the other unilaterally.

### 5.6 Module architecture

The Theravāda vīthi is the only one of the three with a genuine **functional-module** architecture, in which discrete cognitive functions (adverting, sensing, receiving, investigating, determining, impulsing, registering) are realised by *different temporal* tokens. The Sarvāstivāda and Yogācāra disperse these functions across the *caitta* taxonomy that operates *within* every moment. The closest Yogācāra analogue is the layered system of eight vijñānas plus four bhāgas, which is module-like *spatially* (or vertically) rather than temporally.

### 5.7 What each system uniquely explains

- **Theravāda uniquely explains** the *phenomenology of attention capture and processing latency*, the precise *micro-mechanism* of karmic responsibility (only the seven javanas), and why an inattentive but waking mind can briefly fail to "register" an object (*atiparitta* objects truncating before javana).
- **Sarvāstivāda uniquely explains** how memory and prospection can be intentional (their objects exist), how karma acts across temporal gulfs without intermediary storage, and how a single moment can be cognitively complete without requiring a pre-cognitive ramp.
- **Yogācāra uniquely explains** the unity of personality and karmic biography across deep gaps (the ālaya), the apparent solidity of an "external" world (seed-projection), the constitutive self-illusion of ordinary cognition (manas reifying ālaya as ātman), and the structural fact of reflexive self-awareness within a single moment (*svasaṃvitti*).

### 5.8 What problems each system faces

- **Theravāda:** The discrete sequence raises the question of what *causes* the bhavaṅga to vibrate — if an external rūpa cannot directly affect a mental moment, an unbridgeable causal-interaction problem appears at the very threshold. Critics from the Sarvāstivāda already pressed this. The rigid arithmetic of seventeen sub-moments also looks more like a doctrinal commitment than an empirical finding. Furthermore, the rejection of *svasaṃvitti* — no citta can know itself within one moment — leaves the tradition with an awkward account of how cross-moment self-knowledge actually arises.
- **Sarvāstivāda:** The doctrine that past and future dharmas exist seems to undermine momentariness itself. Vasubandhu's own *Bhāṣya* introduces Sautrāntika objections so devastating that he ultimately abandoned the Vaibhāṣika view, paving his transition toward Yogācāra. If a past dharma still exists, in what sense has it ceased? The four sub-theories (bhāva, lakṣaṇa, avasthā, anyathātva) are themselves attempts to repair this tension, none fully convincing. Ronkin (2005, ch. 3) frames the tension as one between dynamic, function-based and substantialist conceptions of *svabhāva*.
- **Yogācāra:** If the apparent object is a seed-projection, what guarantees inter-subjectivity and a shared world? Vasubandhu's *Viṃśatikā* devotes itself to this; the answer — shared karmic conditioning — is elegant but contested. The doubling of self-awareness (*svasaṃvitti-saṃvitti*) to halt regress invites the worry that any *n*-th aspect could require an *n+1*-th; Dharmapāla's response — that the third and fourth aspects mutually witness each other — is logically tight but phenomenologically opaque. Dan Arnold (2012) argues that the *svasaṃvitti* doctrine, while transcendentally compelling, faces real difficulty accounting for the *normativity* of cognition — for being answerable to reasons. Finally, the ālaya itself looks dangerously close to a covert self, a charge raised by Madhyamaka critics from Bhāviveka and Candrakīrti onward.

### 5.9 Phenomenological vs ontological framing

A final way to organise the comparison is by mode of inquiry. **Theravāda** is most naturally read as *psychological-functional*: it asks how mental episodes are structured to explain experience and karma. **Sarvāstivāda** is most naturally read as *ontological-causal*: it asks what must exist, and in what relations, for momentary experience to be possible at all. **Yogācāra** sits between, and is the most contested: Lusthaus (2002) reads it as Buddhist phenomenology, a "critique of consciousness"; Waldron (2003) emphasises its psychological depth via the unconscious ālaya; Sharf (2016) and Arnold (2012) urge that the four aspects and *svasaṃvitti* should *not* be read as first-personal phenomenology in the Husserlian sense but as structural-transcendental posits within a momentariness metaphysics.

## 6. Synthesis: Three Phenomenologies of a Single Glance

When a Theravādin tells the story of someone seeing a mango, the camera moves: bhavaṅga vibrates, attention adverts, the eye sees, reception receives, investigation investigates, determination determines, impulsion runs, registration tastes, bhavaṅga resumes. Karma is born in the running.

When a Vaibhāṣika tells the same story, the camera is still: a single moment of eye-consciousness arises with its ten universal mental factors and whatever wholesome or unwholesome factors are appropriate, conditioned by an immediately-preceding mental moment and a really-existing visible object (whose pastness, presence, or futureness merely determines its mode of existence, not its existence). The next moment arises as samanantara-pratyaya dictates.

When a Yogācārin tells the story, the camera turns inward: the ālaya releases mango-seeds (perfumed by countless prior mango-encounters and mango-words), the manifest vijñāna unfolds into its four bhāgas, the *nimitta* takes the appearance of mango, the *darśana* takes the appearance of seer, the *svasaṃvitti* knows that seeing-mango has occurred, and the manas immediately misappropriates the whole event as "*I* saw a mango," seeding the next round.

All three accounts are anatta-preserving. All three are momentariness-preserving. They diverge — and this is the deepest comparative point — on which philosophical resource bears the explanatory weight: **time** (Theravāda spreads it out), **being** (Sarvāstivāda lets the past linger), or **depth** (Yogācāra folds it inside).
