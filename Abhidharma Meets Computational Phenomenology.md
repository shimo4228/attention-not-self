## Abhidharma meets computational phenomenology: a systematic mapping 

Buddhist Abhidharma dharma classification systems share striking structural parallels with modern computational phenomenology, active inference, and neurophenomenology — but the correspondence is partial, and the deepest disanalogies reveal fundamental differences in purpose, ontology, and scope. The strongest mapping is between manaskāra (attention) and precision-weighting in predictive processing, where functional descriptions are nearly identical. The weakest is any analog to nirvāṇa — the unconditioned element that no computational framework can accommodate. Between these poles lies a rich landscape of structural parallels whose scholarly development ranges from well-established (Thompson, Varela, Ramstead) to almost entirely unexplored (Paṭṭhāna conditional relations mapped to Bayesian causal networks). This report synthesizes the current state of scholarship, identifies genuine correspondences and disanalogies, and presents a comprehensive mapping table enabling further research. 

## The computational phenomenology program and its Buddhist roots 

Computational phenomenology, coined by Maxwell Ramstead and colleagues, uses generative models from computational neuroscience to formalize first-person phenomenological descriptions of lived experience. The 2022 paper “From Generative Models to Generative Passages” (Ramstead et al., _Review of Philosophy and Psychology_ ) establishes its core methodology: first-person phenomenology itself becomes the “data” that generative models explain, providing what Chris Fields calls a “Rosetta Stone” between subjective experience and neurobiological measurement. 

The field descends directly from Francisco Varela’s neurophenomenology (1996), which proposed mutual constraints between first-person phenomenological reports and thirdperson neural data as a methodological remedy for the hard problem of consciousness. Varela explicitly connected phenomenological reduction to Buddhist mindfulness, noting that “this capacity for becoming aware has been paid so little attention as a human pragmatics… as attested in the mindfulness tradition of various Buddhist schools.” Ramstead et al. (2022) acknowledge this lineage directly: “the application of this approach to the phenomenology of Buddhist contemplative practice is an interesting prospect given 

that there exists a rich body of knowledge about the structure of consciousness in the Abhidharma and Yogācāra scriptures.” 

The methodological framework has been refined through five levels (Sandved-Smith et al., 2024/2025): from phenomenological analysis (A1) through multidimensional phenomenological assessment (A2), experimental neurophenomenology (A3), and computational phenomenology (A4) to deep computational neurophenomenology (A5), which uses Bayesian mechanics — described as a “physics of beliefs” — to establish formal circulation across first-person descriptions, mathematical models, and third-person neurophysiological data. The key technical mechanism across all these models is precisionweighting: the confidence attributed to different parameters in hierarchical generative models, which shapes experiential shifts during meditation and other altered states. A 2025 review by Tal, Wright, Prest, Sandved-Smith, and Sacchet (Harvard/MGH) confirms precision-weighting as the common mechanism across all computational models of meditation phenomenology. 

## Three dharma classification systems and their structural logic 

The three major Abhidharma traditions classify experiential reality into distinct dharma systems with importantly different architectures. Understanding their structural differences is essential before mapping them to computational frameworks. 

The Sarvāstivāda system (75 dharmas) organizes reality into five categories: citta (1 consciousness type), caitta (46 mental factors in 6 subgroups), rūpa (11 forms), cittaviprayukta-saṃskāra (14 factors dissociated from mind), and asaṃskṛta (3 unconditioned dharmas). Its distinguishing commitment is temporal eternalism — all dharmas exist in past, present, and future — and each dharma possesses intrinsic nature (svabhāva). 

The Yogācāra system (100 dharmas) retains the five-category structure but expands consciousness to 8 types (adding ālaya-vijñāna and manas), mental factors to 51, nonconcurrent formations to 24, and unconditioned dharmas to 6 (adding tathatā/suchness). Its critical innovation is the storehouse consciousness (ālaya-vijñāna) containing “seeds” (bīja) that shape perception — a structure with remarkable parallels to generative models in predictive processing. 

The Theravāda system (82 dhammas) operates with only four categories, eliminating citta-viprayukta-saṃskāra entirely. It compensates with an extraordinarily detailed classification of consciousness into 89 types (or 121 in expanded form), 52 cetasikas, 28 material forms, and a single unconditioned dhamma (nibbāna). Its unique contributions include the bhavaṅga (life-continuum consciousness), the 17-moment citta-vīthi (cognitive process model), and the 24 conditional relations (paccayas) of the Paṭṭhāna — 

the most elaborate causal analysis in any Abhidharma tradition.

## Where the mappings run deep: attention, valence, and the generative model 

Three correspondences stand out for their depth and scholarly grounding. 

Manaskāra and precision-weighting represent the strongest mapping in the entire literature. In predictive processing, attention literally _is_ precision-weighting — the modulation of gain on prediction error signals that determines which errors drive belief updating (Feldman & Friston, 2010). Manaskāra’s Abhidharma definition — “causing the mind to involve itself with and apprehend its objects” — is a functional description that maps almost exactly onto precision-weighting’s role in making certain prediction errors more salient. Laukkonen and Slagter (2021) made this connection explicit: focused attention (FA) meditation increases precision on one sensory source while open monitoring (OM) meditation equalizes or reduces precision across sources. Ganeri’s _Attention, Not Self_ (2017) provides the philosophical bridge, arguing that in Buddhaghosa’s system attention is not merely one mental factor among many but the central explanatory concept that replaces the self — performing dual roles of selective placing and focal access that correspond to different precision-weighting operations. 

Vedanā and interoceptive inference form the second-strongest mapping. Vedanā’s threefold classification — pleasant, unpleasant, neutral — accompanying every moment of consciousness parallels how interoceptive inference is theorized to accompany all cognitive processing. Anil Seth’s interoceptive inference framework (2013) proposes that subjective feeling states arise from actively-inferred generative models of interoceptive afferents, while Joffily and Coricelli (2013) formalize valence as the rate of change of free energy. Solms and Friston (2018) identify the elemental form of consciousness itself as affect: decreases in expected uncertainty yield pleasure, increases yield unpleasure. The Abhidharma’s insistence that vedanā is _universal_ — present in every citta-moment without 

exception — finds its computational echo in the claim that valence is not an optional add-on but a fundamental dimension of all self-organizing systems minimizing free energy. 

Ālaya-vijñāna and the hierarchical generative model constitute the most architecturally significant correspondence. William Waldron’s foundational _The Buddhist Unconscious_ (2003) demonstrates that the storehouse consciousness’s “seeds” (bīja) function analogously to prior beliefs or learned parameters: they encode past experience, operate subliminally, and shape how future sensory input is interpreted. The bidirectional relationship between ālaya-vijñāna and manifest consciousness — where experience “perfumes” (vāsanā) the storehouse with new seeds while seeds simultaneously generate predictions shaping perception — mirrors the bidirectional flow of top-down predictions and bottom-up prediction errors in predictive coding. Thompson (2014) maps the Yogācāra three-layer architecture (ālaya-vijñāna → manas → mano-vijñāna) onto the distinction between implicit/procedural memory, pre-reflective self-awareness, and working memory/attentional systems. 

## The self-model parallel: manas, citta-vīthi, and kṣaṇa theory 

The Yogācāra manas (7th consciousness) presents a remarkably precise structural parallel with Thomas Metzinger’s phenomenal self-model (PSM). Both are persistent, subliminal processes that generate the sense of being a self without themselves being a self. Manas continuously appropriates experience as “I” and “mine” through four afflictions — self-view, self-love, self-pride, self-delusion — that map onto functional properties of the PSM: the first-person perspective, the sense of ownership, the sense of agency, and identification with model content. Crucially, both are transparent: the organism cannot recognize the constructive process as a construction. Thompson (2014) calls manas “the preattentive mind” providing “the feeling of being a conscious subject.” Deane (2021) proposes within active inference that selfless experiences in meditation correspond to a collapse of this self-model — directly paralleling the Yogācāra claim that manas is “transformed” (āśrayaparāvṛtti) through deep contemplative practice. 

The citta-vīthi model — the 17-moment cognitive process sequence from bhavaṅga through adverting, bare sensation, receiving, investigating, determining, seven javana moments, and two registration moments — maps suggestively onto temporal stages of perception identified in cognitive neuroscience. The progression from bare sensation through feature integration, object recognition, decision, and active response parallels established stages of neural perceptual processing. The javana phase (where karma is created through seven moments of active engagement) may correspond to Global Workspace Theory’s “ignition” — the moment information becomes globally available for report and action. However, the model is strictly serial (one citta at a time), which conflicts 

with the brain’s massive parallel processing, and the fixed number 17 appears doctrinally rather than empirically motivated. 

The kṣaṇa theory — that consciousness consists of discrete momentary events rather than a continuous stream — finds a modern echo in VanRullen and Koch’s (2003) proposal that perception operates in discrete “frames” tied to alpha-band oscillations (~10 Hz, ~100ms frames). White (2018) explicitly acknowledges: “The idea that conscious percepts fall into discrete temporal frames has a long history: something like it appeared over 2000 years ago in Buddhist thinking.” The Sautrāntika’s cinematic model — “consciousness is visualized as a series of fleeting, momentary states, much like how a film is composed of a rapid succession of still images” — anticipates this hypothesis with striking precision. However, Buddhist estimates of kṣaṇa duration (0.13–13 milliseconds) are far faster than modern estimates of perceptual frames (30–400ms), suggesting sub-perceptual granularity that may not correspond to any measurable neural process. 

## Meditation through the lens of predictive processing 

Laukkonen and Slagter’s (2021) “From Many to (N)one” provides the most comprehensive model connecting meditation stages to predictive processing. Their framework places FA, OM, and non-dual (ND) meditation on a single continuum of progressive prediction reduction: 

- Focused attention: Narrows precision-weighting to a chosen object, reducing external distractors by suppressing competing prediction errors 

- Open monitoring: Releases object-specific predictions while maintaining the observerobserved duality — equalizing precision across sensory channels 

- Non-dual awareness: Releases even the subject-object distinction itself, recognized as a conceptual model constructed from past experience — minimizing temporally deep predictive processing entirely 

This framework yields a compelling computational account of insight (prajñā): insights arise through Bayesian model reduction — finding simpler, more parsimonious models using only prior beliefs without new sensory input. Meditation creates conditions for this “fact-free learning” by progressively deconstructing the self-model that sits atop the predictive hierarchy. The narrative self, in this view, is simply the most temporally deep prediction — and as meditation reduces temporal thickness, self-models are unraveled layer by layer. 

Laukkonen et al.‘s (2025) “Contemplative Artificial Intelligence” extends these insights into AI alignment, proposing four axiomatic principles drawn from Buddhist contemplative 

traditions: mindfulness (continuous metacognitive monitoring), emptiness (śūnyatā — relaxation of rigid priors, preventing dogmatic goal fixation), non-duality (dissolving adversarial self-other boundaries), and boundless care (bodhicitta — motivating universal suffering reduction). Empirical testing showed that prompting AI systems to reflect on these principles improved performance on safety benchmarks (d=0.96) and dramatically boosted cooperation in Prisoner’s Dilemma tasks (d=7+). The paper proposes active inference as the natural implementation framework for these principles, since its generative models provide the parameters needed to formalize contemplative insights. 

## Five critical disanalogies that limit all mapping attempts 

The structural correspondences, however genuine, operate within boundaries set by five fundamental disanalogies that any serious comparative project must acknowledge. 

Purpose diverges irreducibly. The Abhidharma’s dharma analysis serves a dual soteriological function identified by Noa Ronkin: evaluative analysis mapping constituents of unwholesome versus awakened consciousness, and descriptive analysis of conditioned experience. What counts as a successful analysis is transformative insight leading to cessation of suffering, not predictive accuracy or explanatory power. Computational frameworks have no equivalent to liberation. 

Ontological commitments differ at the deepest level. Abhidharma dharmas possess svabhāva (intrinsic nature) and are claimed to be ultimately real (paramārtha-sat) — they are property-particulars, not functional descriptions. Computational states are multiply realizable functional descriptions making no ontological claims about ultimate reality. As Siderits notes, “Buddhist reductionism terminates in experiential property-particulars (vedanā, rūpa); cognitive reductionism terminates in physical processes.” Moreover, the Mādhyamika critique goes further than any computational framework: even dharmas lack svabhāva. This represents a level of anti-essentialism that functionalism never reaches. 

The unconditioned has no analog. Nirvāṇa — “unborn, unbecome, unmade, unfabricated” — is classified as asaṃskṛta (unconditioned), beyond dependent arising, change, and temporal limitation. Computational systems are by definition conditioned: every state is determined by prior states and inputs. Even the FEP’s free energy minimum is a conditioned state. As one scholar notes, representational theories like GWT “are generally not concerned with properties of conscious experience like continuity” — and they certainly cannot accommodate a reality beyond all conditioning. 

Enactivism, not computationalism, is the natural partner. Thompson, Varela, and Rosch argued from the beginning that Buddhist philosophy aligns with enactivism against computationalism. Their core argument: cognition is not “the representation of a pre-given 

world by a pre-given mind but is rather the enactment of a world and a mind on the basis of a history of the variety of actions that a being in the world performs.” Buddhist analysis places consciousness (citta) at the center — not as an epiphenomenon of computation but as constitutive of experience. The citta-vīthi’s serial processing assumption and the Abhidharma’s rejection of subpersonal computation are not bugs but features of a system designed to map lived experience, not brain architecture. 

The ethical dimension is irreducible. Every citta-moment in Abhidharma carries ethical valence — wholesome (kusala), unwholesome (akusala), or indeterminate. This ethical dimension is not decorative but causally efficacious through karma. Computational states, priors, and precision parameters are ethically neutral. The ālaya-vijñāna’s seeds carry moral weight; the generative model’s parameters do not. Any mapping that strips away this dimension loses something constitutive of the Abhidharma framework. 

## Structural parallels at the architectural level 

Beyond individual cetasika-to-process mappings, several architectural parallels deserve attention. 

The Abhidharma five-category structure (citta, cetasika, rūpa, citta-viprayukta-saṃskāra, asaṃskṛta) has a partial mapping to cognitive architecture: citta → central executive or global workspace (bare awareness capacity); cetasika → functional cognitive modules operating in conjunction with consciousness; rūpa → sensory/physical substrates. The cittaviprayukta-saṃskāra — conditioned factors that are neither mental nor material, including abstract structural properties like acquisition, life-force, and linguistic capacities — may correspond to what in computational terms would be organizational or architectural principles governing how mind and matter interact. The Theravāda’s elimination of this category in favor of a four-category system parallels reductionist moves in cognitive science that eliminate “faculty psychology” categories in favor of process descriptions. This is closer to reductionism than strict eliminativism: the Theravāda doesn’t deny the phenomena but reanalyzes them as aspects of mind, matter, or their interactions. 

Dependent origination (pratītyasamutpāda) finds its strongest modern parallel in dynamical systems theory and autopoiesis, as Joanna Macy demonstrated in _Mutual Causality in Buddhism and General Systems Theory_ (1991). Both reject linear unidirectional causation for reciprocal, mutual causality; both model reality as process rather than substance; both deny any first cause. The Markov blanket formalism offers a more recent parallel — describing how systems maintain identity through ongoing boundarymaintenance rather than fixed essences — but with an important tension: dependent origination emphasizes the lack of inherent existence of boundaries, while Markov blankets define them. The resolution may lie in the dynamic, nested character of Markov blankets, 

which can be “knitted” and dissolved (Clark, 2017). 

The Paṭṭhāna’s 24 conditional relations represent perhaps the richest unexplored mapping opportunity. These 24 paccayas — including root cause, object condition, predominance, proximity, co-nascence, reciprocity, decisive support, karma, nutriment, faculty, association, and presence/absence conditions — constitute a causal typology far richer than standard Bayesian networks, which recognize essentially one type of causal relation. The Paṭṭhāna’s mutuality condition (aññamañña-paccaya) and co-nascence condition (sahajāta-paccaya) require cyclic or bidirectional causal graphs, while its absence condition (natthi-paccaya) anticipates what contemporary philosophy of science calls causation by omission. No published paper has attempted a formal mapping between these 24 conditions and any computational causal framework — this remains a genuine research frontier. 

## The key researchers and their contributions 

The scholarly landscape organizing this cross-traditional dialogue clusters around several complementary approaches: 

- Evan Thompson ( _The Embodied Mind_ , 1991; _Mind in Life_ , 2007; _Waking, Dreaming, Being_ , 2014): the enactivist bridge, connecting Buddhist philosophy with embodied cognition, autopoiesis, and phenomenology 

- Jonardon Ganeri ( _Attention, Not Self_ , 2017): attention as the central explanatory concept replacing self, bridging Buddhaghosa and cognitive science 

- Jake Davis (with Thompson, 2013): mapping the five aggregates to phenomenal consciousness, access consciousness, and affective processing 

- Christian Coseru ( _Perceiving Reality_ , 2012): Buddhist epistemology (Dignāga, Dharmakīrti) as a theory of cognitive events compatible with enactivism 

- Matthew MacKenzie ( _Buddhist Philosophy and the Embodied Mind_ , 2022): the fullest engagement of 4E cognitive science with Buddhist philosophy, developing a nonreductionist account via autopoiesis 

- Maxwell Ramstead, Lars Sandved-Smith, Antoine Lutz (2021–2025): computational phenomenology using active inference, formally modeling meta-awareness and attentional control in meditation 

- Ruben Laukkonen (with Slagter, 2021; with Inglis, Chandaria et al., 2025): predictive processing model of meditation stages; contemplative AI alignment framework 

- William Waldron ( _The Buddhist Unconscious_ , 2003): ālaya-vijñāna mapped to the cognitive unconscious and implicit memory 

- Christopher deCharms ( _Two Views of Mind_ , 1997): early neuroscientific mapping of Abhidharma perception and mental factor theory, with the critical caveat that “even if the laws within the two systems are similar, they operate over very different domains of experience” 

## Conclusion: a partial Rosetta Stone with principled limits 

The mapping between Abhidharma dharma classification systems and computational phenomenology frameworks reveals a field at an early but promising stage of development. The strongest correspondences — manaskāra/precision-weighting, vedanā/interoceptive inference, ālaya-vijñāna/generative model — are not mere analogies but reflect genuine convergent insights about the structure of conscious experience, arrived at through radically different methods across two millennia. The Abhidharma’s insistence on universal mental factors as minimal conditions for any conscious moment, its analysis of consciousness into discrete temporal units, and its elaborate causal typology all anticipate concerns that computational neuroscience has only recently begun to formalize. 

Yet the mapping is principally limited in three ways. First, the scholarly literature directly comparing Abhidharma categories with computational frameworks at a technical level remains surprisingly thin — most of the specific cetasika-to-process mappings in the table above are structural parallels extrapolated from parallel literatures rather than explicitly argued positions. Second, the enactivist tradition (Varela, Thompson) has consistently argued that Buddhist philosophy aligns with enactivism against computationalism — making active inference attractive precisely to the degree that it can be read as compatible with enactivism (as Ramstead et al. suggest). Third, the Abhidharma’s soteriological purpose, ethical dimensions, and commitment to an unconditioned reality mark hard limits where no computational framework can follow. 

The most promising frontiers for future research include: a formal mapping of the Paṭṭhāna’s 24 paccayas to richly-typed causal graphs; a systematic comparison between IIT’s axioms and the Abhidharma’s universal cetasikas as minimal conditions for consciousness; computational modeling of the citta-vīthi’s temporal dynamics using Global Workspace Theory; and — perhaps most ambitiously — a rigorous formalization of ālaya-vijñāna’s seed theory within the hierarchical generative model framework of active inference. These are not merely academic exercises: they could yield genuinely novel computational architectures inspired by 2,500 years of systematic first-person investigation into the structure of mind.
