# When Consciousness Fully Stops, How Does the Mind Return?

*Two traditions, one question: how does consciousness restart after it fully stops?*

## Why I'm Writing This

Let me confess my position up front. Without it, this essay loses its bearings.

For a long time I've felt that the Buddhist teachings built around meditative practice carry a certain universality. Strip away the trappings of a particular culture or era, and something still seems to be touching the underlying structure of the human mind — or so I suspect.

But I'm also aware that this very intuition rests on a modern product called "Buddhist modernism." That framework — meditation at the core, ritual and cosmology pushed to the margins, Buddhism recast as "a science of the mind" that therefore "doesn't conflict with science" — is itself a fairly recent image of Buddhism, rewoven in the encounter with the West from the nineteenth century onward. So the phrase "Buddhism is scientific" has long made me uncomfortable. I was already taking that kind of talk with a grain of salt well before Evan Thompson put the unease into clean words in *Why I Am Not a Buddhist* (2020). Reaching for science to elevate Buddhism, or reaching for Buddhism to lend science a blessing — both feel like a too-easy sleight of hand.

And I'm Japanese, which adds another twist. In Japan, "Buddhism" first conjures sutras and funerals — something for mourning the dead, its contents vague. Meditation-centered Buddhism — vipassanā, mindfulness — didn't grow straight out of that Japanese Buddhism; it was re-edited once in the West and then reimported. So when I touch it, my sense of it is quite different from a Western practitioner's, and more tangled. It should be continuous with my own culture, yet I'm watching it through someone else's windowpane.

I said "through a windowpane," but I can be more concrete about where I stand. In Japan, the people who seriously study a teaching like Abhidharma fall, roughly, into two lopsided paths. One is the hereditary temple priest who becomes a university Buddhist scholar. The other is the hardcore meditator — formed under reimported Buddhist modernism, having done long retreats in Myanmar or Thailand — together with the lay people who study those teachings at home; what they learn is usually Theravāda Abhidharma (Abhidhamma).

I'm neither. I belong to no sect — a "nightstand Buddhist" who just stacks Buddhist books by the pillow. And what I read is the Abhidharma of the Abhidharmakośa (Sarvāstivāda) and of Yogācāra, written by Japanese specialist scholars — the Northern, Chinese-translation lineage, not Theravāda — and from there I practice meditation on my own. People with this particular combination are, I suspect, almost nonexistent in Japan.

On top of that, as far as I can see, Buddhist scholarship and meditation get along badly in the Japanese meditation scene. Value is placed on meditative "experience," and holding forth on doctrine can even be taken as a sign of low attainment. So I'm half a step off from the scholar-priests and half a step off from the experience-first meditators alike. Wherever I stand, I fall between stools. Graft onto that the scientific discourse of a Thompson or a Laukkonen, and the sense of being a foreign body only thickens.

I won't insist this foreignness is a strength. It just means I have no position to defend. But owing nothing to any camp, I can probably cross the borders between traditions and disciplines without being bound by their etiquette. Whether the view from here is any better, I don't know. At most: having no stake to protect, I can compare the structure of the correspondences coolly — that much, and no more.

This essay is written from that rare, twisted place. Which is exactly why what I want to do is *not* to cry "look, Buddhism predicted neuroscience two thousand years ago" — that is precisely the Buddhist-modernist cliché I've been most wary of. What I want is something cooler. Ancient Buddhism and the modern science of consciousness, from entirely different motives, happened to run into one and the same hard problem and designed different answers to it — I want to trace one concrete instance of that, carefully, keeping both the precision and the limits of the correspondence in view. I'll take my cue from Buddhism's most extreme experience: consciousness coming, completely, to a stop.

## Consciousness That Vanishes Completely — nirodha samāpatti

There are people who can extinguish consciousness completely from the inside, with no drug and no surgical intervention. In Theravāda Buddhism it is called nirodha samāpatti (rendered in Chinese as "the cessation of perception and feeling") — a state in which an advanced meditator halts all experience for up to seven days.

This doesn't happen to just anyone. According to the classical texts (Buddhaghosa's *Visuddhimagga* and others), only a small handful of the most advanced practitioners can enter nirodha samāpatti at will. They must be able to move freely in and out of all eight jhānas (the deepening stages of concentration). They must be deeply accomplished in vipassanā (insight) practice. And they must have reached the stage of the "non-returner" (anāgāmin — one said never again to return to the round of rebirth) or full awakening. Someone who meets those conditions sets an intention beforehand — when, in how many hours, to emerge; not to die in the state — and then quietly enters. Six or seven days at most; beyond that, the texts warn, it grows dangerous for the body. It is unlike anesthesia, and unlike deep sleep. Those who come out report "no sense of time having passed," "no dreams," and cannot be roused by being shaken from outside (Laukkonen et al., 2023).

It's a strange state, but here I want to narrow in on one plain question. **Once consciousness has, literally, gone to zero — how does it "restart"?**

To think about this, I want to share two premises first. One: how science has handled the phenomenon of meditation so far, and where it is now stuck. Two: how Buddhism understood "mind" in the first place. It looks like a detour, but without these two it won't be clear why "restart" is such a thorny question.

## Premise One — Doing Science on Meditation: the Accumulation and the Gap

There is by now a substantial body of scientific research on meditation. But its interest has long leaned in one direction. At its center has been clinical application, typified by MBSR (mindfulness-based stress reduction) — meditation as an intervention to ease stress, anxiety, depression. Alongside it, studies piled up that peered into the brains of long-term practitioners with fMRI and EEG to measure how attention and emotion regulation change. The styles of meditation got sorted out too, described in types: focused attention (gathering attention on a single point), open monitoring (observing broadly without judgment), non-dual (letting go of the subject–object division itself).

But this map had a large blank. **The state in which consciousness itself vanishes — cessation — had been left almost untouched.** No wonder: the practitioners who can produce it are extremely rare, and the brief ones are over in an instant (on the order of milliseconds). Catching and measuring it is technically too hard. What developed most in meditation research was the "tuning attention" kind of practice; the deepest, "vanishing" kind stayed in the margin of the map.

The irony is that this very margin is something the science of consciousness has, in truth, wanted desperately.

Why is an "on/off switch" so important? The science of consciousness runs up against an unavoidable methodological constraint. Consciousness can't be observed directly from outside — however precisely you peer into someone else's brain, "experience itself" doesn't show up there. What shows up is blood flow and electrical activity, not the painfulness of pain or the redness of red. So researchers take an indirect route. **They line up two situations as similar as possible and compare what differs in the brain between "when there's experience" and "when there isn't."** In consciousness research this is called the contrastive method, and the brain activity that surfaces from it is called the "neural correlates of consciousness." The activity that appears and disappears in step with the presence or absence of experience is the leading candidate for the machinery that produces consciousness.

The crucial point here is to **change only consciousness and hold everything else constant.** If, along with the presence or absence of experience, the stimulus being shown, the task being set, the level of arousal, the brain's chemical state all move too — then you can't tell whether the observed brain difference is "due to consciousness" or "due to something else." The ideal is a device that flips consciousness on and off while every other condition stays fixed. Only when the difference between the two situations narrows to "is consciousness present or not" can you attribute that difference to the machinery of consciousness itself. You need the switch because it makes possible an experiment that moves a single variable — consciousness.

But that ideal switch is hard to come by. Anesthesia — the drug itself rewrites the brain's chemistry broadly; you can't separate out whether what vanished was consciousness or the countless other things the drug tampered with. Sleep can't be controlled to a chosen moment, and if you dream, consciousness comes back. Vegetative states and coma vary wildly in the site and degree of damage, can't be predicted or reproduced, and may even leave a faint awareness behind. In every case "consciousness off" comes bundled with "some other large change," and the crucial difference gets muddied.

By one's own intention, with no drug, in an otherwise living and healthy brain, dropping consciousness alone clean away and then returning on one's own — the meditator's cessation comes closer than any other method to that ideal of "everything fixed, only consciousness variable." Which is exactly why it ought to be prized by the science of consciousness — yet it had been left behind in the innermost margin of the research map.

The ones who stepped into this were Laukkonen and colleagues. What they newly brought in was less a novel dataset than a theoretical engine — active inference / predictive processing. It treats the brain as a device that constantly issues predictions and keeps minimizing the misses (prediction errors), a framework with large influence in today's brain science. In 2021, Laukkonen and Slagter recast the stages of meditation — focused attention, open monitoring, non-dual — as a single continuum along which prediction depth gradually thins. Narrow attention to a single point and the excess predictions are suppressed; release attachment to the object and prediction thins further; in the non-dual, even the prediction that "the next moment will be thus" is let go.

On the far end of that line they placed cessation as the extreme. If you thin the depth of prediction all the way to zero — if the very binding that holds experience together as one comes undone — won't consciousness drop away? **Giving language in which the "complete off" that prior meditation research couldn't touch could be stated as a testable hypothesis** — that was Laukkonen and colleagues' contribution. They also add preliminary EEG data: about twenty seconds before cessation, the brain's large-scale connectivity (functional connectivity in the alpha band) begins to fall.

And yet. While the road *into* cessation thus began to be put into words, about what lies beyond — how a consciousness that has completely vanished stands back up again — the paper sets down its pen with this:

> But how does consciousness remerge? This is a key question for future research.

The restart is left blank. Why is this a "key question"? To understand that, we need the second premise — how Buddhism saw the mind.

## Premise Two — There Is No Owner Called "the Self"

We ordinarily picture the mind like this: at the center sits an owner, "I," and that I receives one experience after another. Seeing the scenery, feeling pain, remembering yesterday — all the doing of the same single "I."

Buddhist psychology denies this head-on. Non-self (anattā) — the claim that there is no such entity as a "self" that persists through and across the stream of experience. There is only a chain of mental events arising and passing moment by moment. "Seeing" arises and vanishes. "Feeling" arises and vanishes. The unchanging "I" that owns them all together is nowhere to be found — the more finely you observe experience, the more the owner wastes away. What remains at the end is the stream of events itself.

The one who systematized this view exhaustively, rather than leaving it a religious slogan, is Abhidharma. Abhidharma is, you might say, a cognitive science of 2,500 years ago: it set out to count up one by one the smallest events that make up the mind — dharmas (the irreducible, momentary constituents of the psychological and the physical) — to sort them by kind, and to describe how they condition one another to give rise to the next moment. Not "what is the self" but "what is happening now," written down to the level of components.

From here the system's greatest hard problem rises up. **If there is no owner called the self — if there is only a stream of events, prone to gaps — what is it that joins one moment to the next?** What makes yesterday's me and today's me "the same me"? With no owner, why is there continuity?

Ordinarily this question never surfaces, because the stream never fully stops. Even during deep sleep, a minimal stream of mind keeps flowing — so many Buddhist traditions held. But what if that stream itself were to stop completely? — here the nirodha samāpatti of the opening returns. **nirodha samāpatti is the extreme state in which even that minimal stream is said to be cut.** Which is exactly why the question "if non-self, why continuity?" is pressed home in its sharpest possible form. After seven days with the stream of mind broken off, why do memory, personality, the attainments of practice all continue — why does the same person wake?

## Where Laukkonen Stepped In, and Stopped

The interesting thing is that Laukkonen and colleagues step right up to the edge of this blank — and do so in Buddhist words. The paper describes cessation as a "dis-integration" of consciousness — the binding that holds experience together as one coming undone — and brings out, as its phenomenological counterparts, the five aggregates (khandha: the five heaps that make up body and mind — form, feeling, perception, formations, consciousness), non-self (anattā), and dependent origination (paṭiccasamuppāda). Of dependent origination in particular, they write that meditators associate it with "the stages of how the mind reassembles."

So they themselves touch the very question of restart-as-reassembly: the self comes undone, the constituents of experience scatter, and from there how does the mind build itself back up. And yet they stay within the frame of a single tradition — the Pāli literature of Theravāda — and take pains to disclaim:

> [...] we are not approaching the topic as historians, but as scientists.

A clean boundary. But beyond that line, one more blank is left. The very "reassembly of the mind" they have just touched — that is the problem the Buddhist schools spent centuries arguing out, differing from one another in their answers.

## If Non-Self, Why Does the Same Person Wake? — Four Answers

How to design "continuity without a self." Here the Abhidharma schools parted ways sharply.

- **The Sarvāstivāda** set up a device called prāpti (acquisition — the property of a dharma's belonging to a particular person). It is placed in a third category that is neither mind nor matter — not experience itself, not an object, but a category for describing the relation of belonging, "which dharma belongs to whom": the factors dissociated from mind (citta-viprayukta-saṃskāra). It is, so to speak, a "clasp" fastening dharmas to the individual. Notably, the Sarvāstivāda classifies nirodha samāpatti itself among these dissociated factors too — as a structural point of contact that keeps attributing the state to the person even while consciousness is stopped.
- **The Sautrāntika** disliked a surplus entity like prāpti and set up seeds (bīja) instead. Experience remains within the mental continuum as dormant potential, and sprouts again when conditions align. Even if consciousness breaks off, the seeds do not.
- **Yogācāra** crystallized this into the ālaya-vijñāna (the store-consciousness). Even when the surface six consciousnesses stop, this deep consciousness — storing past experience as seeds — keeps flowing, and reconstructs the surface consciousness on emergence from cessation.
- And **Theravāda** itself had the bhavaṅga (the "life-continuum"), a baseline mind that keeps flowing even while consciousness is lowered. Take only its function and it is remarkably close to the ālaya-vijñāna — both are "the carrier of continuity that does not break off even when surface consciousness drops."

Line them up and a pattern emerges: these start to look less like theological nitpicking and more like **four different architectures for one design problem — "without a permanent self, what joins moment to moment?"** Put the clasp in a separate category (Sarvāstivāda), fold it into the continuum as potential (Sautrāntika), erect a deep storehouse (Yogācāra), or grant a baseline stream (Theravāda). To the same question, four solutions are given.

## Four Blueprints, Beside the Open Question

The blank Laukkonen and colleagues left by writing "future research" has exactly this shape. How the mind reassembles after cessation — the candidate structures of continuity, Abhidharma had already drawn out concretely, in four ways. When the science of consciousness goes at this question at the neural level, the ancient typology can supply a vocabulary of hypotheses to test — the very way of framing the question: is what carries the restart a localized store, a potential dispersed through the continuum, or a clasp-like structure that preserves attribution?

And — here is the interesting part — this question has the same shape outside the frame of meditation and Buddhism, too. **How does a system with no permanent self nonetheless keep continuity?** Structurally, it is the question facing any AI agent whose session ends and whose context is wiped clean — what to carry over, and what to reconstruct when it next wakes up. The resemblance to the Yogācāra debate over the ālaya-vijñāna is uncanny. Whether it is actually the right frame for an artificial mind, I don't know — but that, after all, is what "attention, not self" was always pointing at: not a self as substance, but attention and a stream of states arising and vanishing moment by moment.

## A Line Not to Cross

Finally, a word of caution. These are structural analogies, not identifications.

First, the very phrasing "cessation is the complete absence of consciousness" needs to be handled with care. Laukkonen and colleagues do indeed distinguish cessation from the "plenitude" of pure consciousness or non-dual awareness, characterizing it rather as the "absence" of awareness. But that characterization rests on practitioners reporting afterward that they "remember nothing." Absence of report, though, does not prove absence of experience (the paper itself admits it is unsettled how far cessation actually interrupts the brain's processing). And, ironically, the four answers we've just seen — the ālaya-vijñāna, the bhavaṅga — are precisely claims that "even when surface awareness vanishes, something at the base does not break off." "Cessation = complete nothing," too, is not a settled fact but one position.

On top of that, between the ālaya-vijñāna and the brain's generative model "resembling" each other and the two being "the same thing," there is a line not to cross. The dharmas of Abhidharma claim inherent nature (svabhāva) and ultimate reality, and bear ethical causation through karma — bearing what the parameters of a computational model do not.

And yet, I think. More than two thousand years ago, someone asked in earnest, "if there is no self, why is there continuity?" — and designed four answers. That question is still open, even in the modern laboratory. I don't mean to plant a flag there reading "Buddhism was right" — that gesture is exactly what I was wary of at the outset. It's just that two inquiries, having peered into the same abyss from different motives, now happen to lie side by side. I'd like to watch that coincidence for a while.
