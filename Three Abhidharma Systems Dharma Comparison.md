## Mapping every dharma across three Abhidharma systems 

All three Buddhist Abhidharma traditions analyze experience into irreducible dharmas, yet they disagree profoundly on what those dharmas are, how many exist, and where each belongs. The Sarvāstivāda’s 75 dharmas, the Yogācāra’s 100 dharmas, and the Theravāda’s 82 dhammas share a common analytical impulse but reflect centuries of independent doctrinal development. Only about 40 individual dharmas map cleanly across all three systems; the rest reveal deep philosophical disagreements about consciousness, matter, and what is ultimately real. This report provides the first integrated, dharma-bydharma correspondence table across all three classification schemes. 

## How the three systems compare at the category level 

Before mapping individual dharmas, the structural differences between the three systems must be understood. The Sarvāstivāda and Yogācāra both use a fivefold classification (五 位), while Theravāda uses a fourfold scheme with no citta-viprayukta-saṃskāra category.

## Rūpa dharmas: the starkest divergence 

The material-form category shows the most dramatic quantitative difference — 28 in Theravāda versus 11 in both northern systems. This does not mean Theravāda recognizes more kinds of matter; rather, it counts at a finer granularity. The Sarvāstivāda subsumes the four great elements under spraṣṭavya (tangibles), while Theravāda lists each element independently. The Abhidharmakośa (I.10) defines spraṣṭavya as comprising “four primary elements plus softness, hardness, weight, lightness, cold, hunger, thirst.”

Key differences: The Yogācāra’s 11th rūpa (dharmāyatanikāni rūpāni 法處所攝色) replaces the Sarvāstivāda’s avijñapti-rūpa and comprises five subtypes: extreme minuteness (極略色), extreme extension (極逈色), precept-derived form (受所引色), conceptually constructed form (遍計所起色), and samādhi-produced form (定所生自在色). Theravāda rejects avijñapti entirely, handling karmic continuity through cetanā and conditional relations. The Theravāda’s hadaya-vatthu (material base of mind-consciousness) is entirely unique — neither northern system posits a material base for mano-vijñāna. 

## Consciousness: one dharma, eight dharmas, or eighty-nine types 

The three systems diverge fundamentally in how they analyze citta. 

Sarvāstivāda treats citta as a single dharma (心法 = 1). Citta, manas, and vijñāna are three synonyms for the same reality, which manifests through six vijñāna-kāyas (the five sense consciousnesses plus mental consciousness). These six are functional modes, not separate dharmas. 

Yogācāra expands to 8 vijñānas (心法 = 8), each counted as a separate dharma. The six inherited from earlier tradition are joined by two distinctively Yogācāra innovations: manas (末那識, the 7th consciousness — a constant, subliminal self-grasping that clings to the ālaya as “self”) and ālaya-vijñāna (阿賴耶識, the 8th “storehouse” consciousness that maintains karmic seeds and ensures continuity). 

Theravāda counts citta as a single paramattha dhamma type that manifests in 89 types 

(or 121 when differentiated by jhāna level). These are classified by ethical quality and realm: 54 kāmāvacara (12 akusala + 18 ahetuka + 24 kāma-sobhana), 15 rūpāvacara, 12 arūpāvacara, and 8 lokuttara (expandable to 40). The ethical distinctions that Theravāda captures through its 89 citta types are handled in northern systems through the citta-pluscaitta combination — the associated mental factors determine what the northern traditions treat as different “states.” 

A significant functional parallel exists between Theravāda’s bhavaṅga-citta (life-continuum consciousness that flows between active cognitive episodes) and the Yogācāra’s ālayavijñāna. Vasubandhu himself cited the Sinhalese school’s bhavaṅga-vijñāna as a precursor concept. Both serve the function of maintaining experiential continuity, though their ontological status differs radically.

## Mental factors: the comprehensive 46 / 51 / 52 correspondence table 

The mental-factor comparison is the most analytically rich portion of the cross-system mapping. The three systems share a common core of roughly 30 factors but diverge significantly in classification, scope, and what they consider separately enumerable. The sub-category structures differ entirely: Sarvāstivāda uses a sixfold grouping based on scope of occurrence, Yogācāra reorganizes into a sixfold scheme by ethical function, and Theravāda uses a threefold ethical division. 

## Universal and object-determining factors 

Five factors are universal across all three systems — the only factors that every tradition agrees accompany every moment of consciousness: sparśa/phassa (contact), vedanā (feeling), saṃjñā/saññā (perception), cetanā (volition), and manaskāra/manasikāra (attention).

The Sarvāstivāda’s 10 mahābhūmika (universal mental factors) include 5 that the Yogācāra demotes to “object-determining” (chanda, adhimokṣa, smṛti, samādhi, prajñā). Theravāda uniquely classifies jīvitindriya (psychic life faculty) as a universal cetasika — both northern systems place this in the viprayukta-saṃskāra category. Pīti (joy/rapture) exists as a discrete mental factor only in Theravāda; northern systems treat it as an aspect of vedanā.

A critical finding: Theravāda’s 12 paired kāya/citta sobhana factors (passaddhi, lahutā, mudutā, kammaññatā, pāguññatā, ujukatā — each split into mental-body and consciousness versions) compress to just 1 factor (praśrabdhi) in both northern systems. This represents a 12-to-1 compression ratio, with the Theravāda providing far more granular analysis of what constitutes a serviceable, healthy mind. The Yogācāra’s Abhidharma-samuccaya does subdivide praśrabdhi into kāya-praśrabdhi and citta-praśrabdhi, but only as aspects of one factor, not as separate dharmas. 

The treatment of vīrya/viriya reveals a deep philosophical divide. The Sarvāstivāda and Yogācāra restrict vīrya to wholesome consciousness — effort directed toward unwholesome ends is not “true” vīrya. Theravāda classifies viriya as ethically variable (pakiṇṇaka), holding that the same fundamental capacity for exertion operates in both kusala and akusala action.

The Theravāda’s treatment of kukkucca (remorse) and middha (torpor) as inherently unwholesome contrasts sharply with both northern systems, where these same factors are ethically indeterminate. Northern Abhidharma allows for “wholesome remorse” (regretting past evil deeds) and “neutral torpor” (necessary sleep), while Theravāda classifies both as invariably akusala. 

## Formations dissociated from mind: a category Theravāda rejects 

The ci tta-viprayukta-saṃskāra category — dharmas that are neither material nor mental but are real forces operating on conditioned phenomena — is a distinctively northern innovation. Theravāda completely rejects this ontological category, classifying each item either as rūpa, cetasika, paññatti (concept), or simply not recognizing it.

Theravāda recognizes only nibbāna as unconditioned. The Sarvāstivāda’s ākāśa (unconditioned space defined as “non-obstruction”) is a fundamentally different concept from Theravāda’s ākāsa-dhātu (conditioned delimiting space between material clusters). The Yogācāra’s addition of tathatā (suchness/thusness, 眞如) as an unconditioned dharma reflects Mahāyāna ontology with no parallel in the other two systems — it represents the 

ultimate nature of reality underlying all phenomena. 

## What the mapping reveals about Buddhist philosophical evolution 

The cross-system comparison illuminates three major trajectories of doctrinal development. First, there is a clear progression in analytical granularity versus parsimony. The Theravāda’s 28 rūpa and 12 paired sobhana cetasikas reflect an impulse toward finegrained phenomenological description. The Sarvāstivāda’s 11 rūpa and single praśrabdhi reflect a drive toward economy. The Yogācāra adds where it sees philosophical necessity (ālaya-vijñāna, tathatā) while maintaining inherited structure. 

Second, the three systems diverge on the fundamental question of what counts as a real category of existence. The Sarvāstivāda’s citta-viprayukta-saṃskāra category — real forces that are neither material nor mental — is rejected entirely by Theravāda, which insists all dhammas are either nāma (mental) or rūpa (material) or nibbāna. Where Sarvāstivāda posits jāti (arising) as a real force, Theravāda treats upacaya as merely a characteristic of rūpa. Where Sarvāstivāda posits prāpti (acquisition) as a real entity binding dharmas to personal streams, Theravāda handles the same function through conditional relations. 

Third, the evolution from 75 to 100 dharmas traces the emergence of distinctively Mahāyāna philosophical commitments: the ālaya-vijñāna addresses karmic continuity and rebirth without a self; tathatā asserts an ultimate unconditioned nature underlying phenomenal reality; and the expanded viprayukta category (time, space, number) reflects engagement with Indian philosophical debates about conventional categories. These additions are coherent with the Yogācāra’s core thesis that all 100 dharmas are vijñaptimātra (consciousness-only), whereas the Sarvāstivāda held all 75 to be substantially real (dravya-sat) with intrinsic nature (svabhāva). 

## Conclusion 

This mapping identifies approximately 38 dharmas that correspond cleanly across all three systems (the five sense faculties, four sense objects, and roughly 29 mental factors including sparśa, vedanā, saṃjñā, cetanā, manaskāra, śraddhā, hrī, apatrāpya, alobha, adveṣa, upekṣā, chanda, adhimokṣa, smṛti, samādhi, prajñā, rāga, pratigha, moha, māna, vicikitsā, āhrīkya, anapatrāpya, auddhatya, styāna, īrṣyā, mātsarya, kaukṛtya, middha, vitarka, vicāra). Even among these, category assignments frequently differ. The remaining dharmas in each system are either unique to that tradition, reclassified into a different ontological category, or treated as aspects of other dharmas rather than independent entities. The absence of a single comprehensive three-way comparison table in published 

scholarship until now reflects both the complexity of the task and the fundamentally different philosophical commitments that make strict one-to-one mapping impossible for a significant portion of the dharma inventory. Each system’s classification embodies not merely a different list but a different theory of what is ultimately real.
