# The 念 Collision: How One Chinese Character Holds Four Buddhisms Apart

## TL;DR

The single character 念 (*nen* / *niàn*) carries at least four incompatible meanings across Buddhist traditions — Theravāda "non-floating recollection" (*sati* / *smṛti*), Chan/Zen "thought-moment" or "thought to be transcended" (as in 無念), Pure Land "vocal recitation / recollection of the Buddha" (念仏), and modern psychological "non-judgmental present-moment awareness" — and because the same graph encodes all four, practitioners moving between traditions routinely import the wrong content under the right-looking word.

The pivot of the collision is Huineng's 無念 (*wúniàn* / *munen*). In the *Platform Sutra* it is defined positively as "not to think even when involved in thought" (Yampolsky §17) — i.e., non-attachment within thinking — but because by the Tang the character 念 had drifted toward "thought / mental moment," 無念 reads to a naïve eye as "no-mindfulness," the apparent negation of Theravāda *sati*. The two traditions look like opposites only because the character has shifted underneath them.

Dōgen's 非思量 (*hi-shiryō*) is the most defensible resolution. It refuses both the cultivate-念 reading (naïve *sati*) and the eliminate-念 reading (naïve *munen*) and points at a third register — thinking that is not structured by the thinking / not-thinking dichotomy — which lines up well with what Gethin, Dreyfus, and Anālayo have argued *sati* actually does in the Pāli sources, and badly with the Kabat-Zinn "bare attention" formula.

## Key Findings

- **Theravāda *sati* is a retentive / structuring faculty, not "bare attention."** The Pāli commentarial tradition (*Milindapañha*, *Visuddhimagga*, *Aṭṭhasālinī*) defines *sati* by its characteristic of *apilāpana* — "not floating away" / "calling-to-mind" — and its function of *asammoha* (non-confusion). It is a *sobhana* (beautiful / wholesome) *cetasika* in Theravāda and a *viniyata* (object-determining) *caitta* in Yogācāra / Sarvāstivāda. Gethin (2011), Dreyfus (2011), Anālayo (2003), and Bhikkhu Bodhi all stress that *sati* derives from √*smṛ*, "to remember," and that the modern equation *sati* = "non-judgmental present-moment awareness" is a 20th-century reformulation traceable through Nyanaponika's *The Heart of Buddhist Meditation* (Rider & Co., London, 1962) to Kabat-Zinn's MBSR program (founded 1979).

- **The Chinese translation 念 was already overloaded before Buddhism arrived.** The graph is composed of 今 (now) + 心 (heart-mind); Karlgren and the *Shuowen jiezi* gloss it as "to have something present to the mind." Pre-Buddhist senses included "to think of," "to recite," "to remember," "to dwell on." Kumārajīva (4th–5th c.) and later Xuanzang (7th c.) chose 念 to render *smṛti* — and built compounds like 念處 (*smṛtyupasthāna*), 念息 (*ānāpāna-smṛti*), and 正念 (*samyak-smṛti* / *sammā-sati*). But because 念 already meant "a thought" in ordinary Chinese, the translation could not prevent the term from being read as "thought-moment" — an ambiguity that does not exist in Pāli, where *sati* and *vitakka* / *vicāra* are clearly different *cetasikas*.

- **Huineng's 無念 means non-attachment in thought, not absence of thought.** In Yampolsky's translation of the Dunhuang *Platform Sutra* §17, the triad is set out explicitly: "no-thought as the main doctrine [無念為宗], non-form as the substance [無相為體], non-abiding as the basis [無住為本]"; 無念 is glossed as 於念而不念 — "not to think even when involved in thought" — and 於一切境上不染 — "to be unstained in all environments." Section 26 makes the dynamic clear: 前念迷即凡、後念悟即佛 — "When past thoughts are deluded, this is the common man; when future thoughts are awakened to, this is Buddha" (Yampolsky 1967, p. 148). Here 念 is unmistakably "thought-moment," not "mindfulness."

- **By the 8th century, "mindfulness" itself becomes a target of Chan critique.** Robert Sharf's "Mindfulness and Mindlessness in Early Chan" (*Philosophy East and West* 64, no. 4 [October 2014]: 933–964, DOI 10.1353/pew.2014.0074) documents that early Chan manuscripts "are relentless in their critiques of 'maintaining mind' (*shouxin*), 'viewing mind' (*guanxin*), and indeed of 'mindfulness' (*nian*) itself" (p. 945); against 守心 they set 無心 (*wuxin*), against 觀心 they set 絕觀 (*jueguan*), and against 念 they set 無念. Sharf reads this as a quasi-deconstructive critique of the reification of mind — not a brief for blankness, but a refusal to convert *sati* into a substantialist object of cultivation.

- **The same character thus does opposite work in the two traditions.** In Theravāda the practitioner is told to *cultivate* 念 (*sati*); in Chan the practitioner is told to *transcend* 念 (now meaning conceptual thought). The character is identical; the semantic load has rotated. This is why D.T. Suzuki could write that "*Mushin* [*wu-hsin*] or *munen* [*wu-nien*] is one of the most important ideas in Zen," treating 無念 as essentially synonymous with 無心 — and not as the negation of Pāli *sati*.

- **Pure Land preserves the memory pole of *smṛti* that Zen lost.** *Buddhānusmṛti* (recollection of the Buddha) was translated as 念佛 (Ch. *niànfó*, Jp. *nenbutsu*). In its earliest forms this was contemplative — keeping the Buddha in mind — and on Mark Blum and John Keenan's analyses it preserves the linear, memorial, *anussati*-related sense of *smṛti*. Hōnen and Shinran shifted the practice from contemplation toward vocal recitation (*shōmyō nenbutsu*), but the 念 of 念仏 still resonates with "remembrance" rather than "thought-flash." A Japanese reader thus encounters at least three live senses of 念 — *sati*-style 正念, Zen-style "thought-flash," and Pure Land "calling-to-mind / recitation" — without typographic warning.

- **Dōgen's 非思量 is a structural escape, not just a slogan.** In *Shōbōgenzō Zazenshin* and *Fukanzazengi*, Dōgen quotes Yaoshan: 思量箇不思量底。不思量底、如何思量。非思量 ("Think of not-thinking. How do you think of not-thinking? Nonthinking" — Waddell & Abe, "Dōgen's *Fukanzazengi* and *Shōbōgenzō Zazengi*," *The Eastern Buddhist*, n.s. 6, no. 2 [1973]: 115–128, at p. 122). 不思量 (*fushiryō*) is ordinary negation: "not-thinking." 非思量 (*hishiryō*) is structural negation: the predicate "thinking / not-thinking" itself does not apply. This is neither cultivate-念 nor eliminate-念 nor observe-念; it is the refusal of the binary on which the trilemma rests.

- **The modern Japanese terminology compounds the mess.** Japanese Wikipedia and dictionaries now offer at least three Japanese renderings of English "mindfulness": マインドフルネス (transliteration), 念 (*nen*, the classical term — which immediately re-activates the Zen / Pure Land overload), and 気づき (*kizuki*, "noticing / realization," a newly recruited term with no Buddhist pedigree). 気づき was used in the Japanese title of Bhante Gunaratana's *Mindfulness in Plain English* (『マインドフルネス：気づきの瞑想』), explicitly framing *sati* as 気づき. This severs the term from 念 and from Zen, but introduces yet another ambiguity, because 気づき in ordinary Japanese means "noticing" or "epiphany," not a trained faculty.

## Details

### Part 1: 念 in Early Buddhism — *sati* / *smṛti* as retention, not bare attention

The Pāli word *sati* and its Sanskrit cognate *smṛti* both derive from the root √*smṛ*, "to remember." In Vedic usage *smṛti* designated the second-tier corpus of remembered texts (as against *śruti*, "heard" revelation). Early Buddhist usage retains this etymology: a standard sutta formula glosses one with *sati* as "one who remembers and recalls what was said and done long ago" (e.g., SN 48.9; see also AN, MN parallels).

The Abhidharmic refinement clarifies what *sati* does as a discrete *cetasika* (mental factor). The *Milindapañha* lists two characteristics: *apilāpana-lakkhaṇā* — "not floating away" or "calling to mind" — and *upagaṇhana-rasa* — "taking hold." Buddhaghosa's *Visuddhimagga* (XIV.141) and the *Aṭṭhasālinī* repeat *apilāpana* as the defining mark. Gethin emphasises that this is the language of retention: *sati*'s job is to keep an object from drifting from awareness, not to "bare-witness" it (Gethin, "On Some Definitions of Mindfulness," *Contemporary Buddhism* 12.1, 2011). Bhikkhu Bodhi, Dreyfus, and Anālayo all converge on the same reading: *sati* is the retentive / structuring faculty that makes both present-moment awareness and recollection possible. Dreyfus puts it sharply in "Is Mindfulness Present-Centered and Nonjudgmental?" (2011): "the identification of mindfulness with bare attention ignores or, at least, underestimates the cognitive implications of mindfulness, its ability to bring together various aspects of experience so as to lead to the clear comprehension of the nature of mental and bodily states."

*Sati* is classified as a *kusala* / *sobhana cetasika* in Theravāda Abhidhamma — i.e., a wholesome mental factor — and as one of the *viniyata caittas* (object-determining mental factors) in Sarvāstivāda / Yogācāra Abhidharma. *Sammā-sati* (Pāli) / *samyak-smṛti* (Sanskrit), the seventh limb of the Noble Eightfold Path, is ethically directional: it is *sati* conditioned by Right View, deployed within *satipaṭṭhāna* (the four establishings of mindfulness — body, feelings, mind, *dhammas*) and oriented to liberation. The point is decisive: in the canonical-commentarial framework, "mindfulness" is not affect-neutral and not free-floating. It is structured, directional, and trainable.

### Part 2: 念 in Chinese Buddhist translation — the inherited ambiguity

The Chinese graph 念 is decomposed by the *Shuowen jiezi* as 今 (now) + 心 (heart / mind), with Karlgren glossing the etymology as "to have 今 present to 心 the mind" — i.e., having something present in mind. Pre-Buddhist literary uses already span "to think of," "to remember," "to recite," "to dwell on a thought." This semantic spread is wider than that of any single Pāli or Sanskrit term: 念 already covered ground occupied separately in Indic by *smṛti* (memory), *cintā* / *vitarka* (thought, deliberation), and *anusmṛti* (recollection).

When Kumārajīva (鳩摩羅什, 344–413) and his Chang'an translation bureau began rendering Sarvāstivāda meditation texts in the early 5th century, they adopted 念 as the standard equivalent for *smṛti*. Kumārajīva coined or stabilised the compounds 念處 (*niànchù*) for *smṛtyupasthāna*, "establishing of mindfulness"; 念息 for *ānāpāna-smṛti*, "breathing-mindfulness"; and 正念 (*zhèngniàn*) for *samyak-smṛti*, "right mindfulness." Xuanzang (玄奘, 602–664), working two and a half centuries later in a more philological register, retained 念 for *smṛti* and 正念 for *samyak-smṛti*, with little change.

The price of this choice — invisible at the time — was that 念 in Chinese could now do double duty. In a Buddhist register, 念 was the technical equivalent of *smṛti*. In ordinary Chinese, 念 continued to mean "a thought," "to recite," "to remember." Compounds piled up on both sides: 念頭 (*niàntou*) "thought, idea, intention"; 觀念 (*guānniàn*) "concept"; 懷念 (*huáiniàn*) "to cherish the memory of"; 念書 (*niànshū*) "to study / read aloud"; 念經 (*niànjīng*) "to recite sutras"; 念佛 (*niànfó*) "to recite / chant the Buddha's name." A reader of medieval Chinese Buddhist literature could never be certain in advance whether a given 念 meant "*sati*," "thought," "recollection," or "recitation"; only context could fix it.

In Pāli, by contrast, *sati* is a clearly demarcated *cetasika* distinct from *vitakka* (applied thought), *vicāra* (sustained thought), *cintā* (cogitation), and *anussati* (recollection). The translator's economy of using one Chinese graph for what Pāli distinguished by separate technical terms collapsed those distinctions on the receiving end. This is the structural fault line on which the entire later collision rests.

### Part 3: 念 and 無念 in Chan / Zen

#### 3a. Huineng and the *Platform Sutra*

The Dunhuang recension of the *Platform Sutra of the Sixth Patriarch* (*Liùzǔ Tánjīng* 六祖壇經; ed. / trans. Philip B. Yampolsky, Columbia University Press, 1967) sets out the central triad in §17:

> 我自法門，從上已來，頓漸皆立無念為宗，無相為體，無住為本。
>
> "In this teaching of mine, from ancient times up to the present, all have set up no-thought as the main doctrine [無念為宗], non-form as the substance [無相為體], and non-abiding as the basis [無住為本]." (Yampolsky 1967, p. 137)

Huineng then offers the formal definition that has done so much subsequent work:

> 無相者，於相而離相；無念者，於念而不念……於一切境上不染，名為無念。
>
> "Non-form is to be separated from form even when associated with form. No-thought is not to think even when involved in thought… To be unstained in all environments is called no-thought." (Yampolsky 1967, p. 138)

This formulation is decisive for the entire later trajectory. 無念 is **not** the cessation of mental activity. It is a mode of relation to arising thought: thoughts arise, are seen, and pass without grasping. Peter Gregory and Yanagida Seizan both emphasise that the triad 無念 / 無相 / 無住 is best read as three angles on one practice — non-fixation. Ming Dong Gu ("The Dao of No-Thinking," *Dao* 22, 2023) argues that this formulation, with its Daoist resonances (*wuwei*, *zuowang*), is Huineng's most original philosophical move.

The dynamic register is made explicit in §26, where 念 unambiguously means "thought-moment":

> 善知識，煩惱即是菩提。前念迷即凡，後念悟即佛。
>
> "Good friends, the very passions are themselves enlightenment (*bodhi*). When past thoughts are deluded, this is the common man; when future thoughts are awakened to, this is Buddha." (Yampolsky 1967, p. 148)

In one passage, then, 念 carries the technical Chan sense — a *kṣaṇa*-like thought-instant — entirely distinct from *sati*. The same character, four sections later, designates the practice-target of non-fixation upon such thought-instants.

#### 3b. The semantic rotation

In Pāli Abhidhamma, "mindfulness" and "a thought" are different *cetasikas*. In Tang Chan, 念 names both, and 無念 names freedom from the second sort. The result is that the same character that in a Theravāda translation context cues "cultivate it!" cues, in a Chan context, "see through it!" — and the practitioner reading both traditions in Chinese (or in Japanese) has no graphic warning that the term has rotated.

Robert Sharf's "Mindfulness and Mindlessness in Early Chan" (*Philosophy East and West* 64, no. 4 [October 2014]: 933–964) shows that early Chan manuscripts (the *Wuxin lun*, *Jueguan lun*, Northern / Southern Chan polemical texts) push this rotation systematically:

> "There are a number of eighth-century Chan manuscripts that are relentless in their critiques of 'maintaining mind,' 'viewing mind,' and indeed of 'mindfulness' (*nian*) itself. In direct opposition to the injunction to 'maintain mind' (*shouxin*), these texts speak of 'no mind' (*wuxin* 無心); instead of 'to discern mind' (*guanxin*), we find 'cut off discernment' (*jueguan* 絕觀); rather than 'mindfulness,' we find 'no mindfulness' (*wunian* 無念)." (Sharf 2014, p. 945)

Zongmi (宗密, 780–841) preserves the corresponding rhetorical anecdote: "Vimalakīrti ridiculed quiet sitting. Heze [Shenhui] rejected 'freezing the mind.' When Caoqi [Huineng] saw someone sitting cross-legged he would take his staff and beat him until he got up" (Zongmi, *Chanyuan zhuquanji duxu*, T.2015, 48.401b; cited Sharf 2014, p. 934). Sharf reads this not as anti-meditative quietism but as a deconstructive critique of any reified "mindfulness as a thing to grasp": "even notions such as 'mindfulness' and 'maintaining unity' must be abandoned" (Sharf 2014, p. 951).

#### 3c. Linji, Dahui, and koan practice

Song-dynasty Linji-line Chan, especially as systematised by Dahui Zonggao (大慧宗杲, 1089–1163) under the rubric of *kanhua chan* 看話禪 ("investigating-the-word Chan"), turns 念 in yet another direction. The *huatou* (話頭) — for instance the famous *wú* 無 — is to be held in concentrated "doubt" (大疑) until conceptual thinking breaks. In this register 一念 *yīniàn* / *ichinen* (one thought-moment) names both the unit of conceptual mind to be transcended and, in some formulations, the moment of breakthrough. The Linji rhetoric is therefore not "cultivate *sati*" but "cut through 念." Koan practice is **not** mindfulness practice in the Theravāda sense; whether it is "mindful" at all depends on which meaning of 念 one starts from.

#### 3d. Dōgen and *shikantaza*

Dōgen (道元, 1200–1253), in the Sōtō line via Tiantong Rujing, treats the 念 / 無念 problem with unusual analytic care. *Shikantaza* 只管打坐 ("just sitting") is glossed not as no-thought-blankness but through the Yakusan dialogue (preserved in *Jingde chuandeng lu* and made central in Dōgen's *Shōbōgenzō Zazenshin* and *Fukanzazengi*):

> 兀兀坐定、思量箇不思量底。不思量底、如何思量。非思量、此乃坐禪之要術也。
>
> "Think of not-thinking. How do you think of not-thinking? Nonthinking. This in itself is the essential art of *zazen*." (Waddell & Abe, "Dōgen's *Fukanzazengi* and *Shōbōgenzō Zazengi*," *The Eastern Buddhist* 6.2 [1973]: 115–128, at p. 122; also Tanahashi, *Treasury of the True Dharma Eye*, Shambhala 2010)

Dōgen's grammatical knife: 思量 *shiryō* is thinking. 不思量 *fushiryō* is its ordinary negation, "not-thinking." 非思量 *hishiryō* is *structural* negation — the category "thinking / not-thinking" does not apply. Hee-Jin Kim (*Dōgen on Meditation and Thinking*, SUNY Press, 2007) reads 非思量 as "thinking that is non-objectifying" — neither the cultivation of thought nor its suppression, but a mode of activity outside the discursive frame. The parallelism with Huineng's 無念 (於念而不念) is structural: both moves reject the simple negation of 念 in favour of a non-binary mode.

#### 3e. Japanese Zen usage and the everyday register

In modern Japanese Zen — and in everyday Japanese — 念 functions overwhelmingly in the "thought" register, not in the *sati* register: 念を起こす *nen wo okosu* "to give rise to a thought"; 妄念 *mōnen* "deluded thought"; 雑念 *zatsunen* "miscellaneous / distracting thoughts in meditation"; 一念 *ichinen* "one thought-moment." When a Japanese Zen teacher tells students to drop 雑念, they are talking about *distractive thinking* — they are not telling students to abandon *sati*. But the character on the page is the same character that, in 正念 *shōnen*, names *sammā-sati*.

Pure Land usage runs orthogonal to this: 念仏 *nenbutsu* preserves the *anussati* / recollection sense, and after Hōnen and Shinran is overwhelmingly vocal-recitative. As Mark Blum and Dennis Hirota have emphasised, Shinran's 念仏 is so far from "having a thought of the Buddha" that he construes it as the Buddha's own working (*tariki*) manifesting in the saying. The 念 of 念仏 and the 念 of 雑念 are doing wildly different jobs.

### Part 4: 念仏 — the third sense

The Sanskrit *buddhānusmṛti* ("recollection of the Buddha") was originally one of a set of *anussati* practices listed in early texts: contemplation of the Buddha's qualities, the Dharma, the Saṅgha, etc. In Indian Mahāyāna it became associated with visualisation of buddhas — especially Amitābha / Amitāyus — in *pratyutpanna-samādhi* practice. The Chinese rendering 念佛 (*niànfó*) carried that sense across, while leaving open whether 念 here meant "recollect," "contemplate," or "recite."

In China and then Japan, the recitation pole won. Shandao (善導, 613–681) systematised the equation of 念佛 with vocal *shōmyō*. Hōnen (法然, 1133–1212), in *Senchakushū* (1198), declared the recitative *nenbutsu* the only practice adequate for the *mappō* age. Shinran (親鸞, 1173–1263) radicalised the move: the *nenbutsu* is no longer a cause of birth in the Pure Land, but its sign — the working of Amida's vow uttering itself through us. Thomas Kasulis (*Zen Action / Zen Person*, and the analysis in *Engaging Japanese Philosophy*) describes Shinran's move as a "dehistoricizing of practice": for Hōnen the *nenbutsu* is a merit-producing engine, for Shinran a pilot light indicating one is plugged in to Other Power.

The key point for the present argument: in 念仏, 念 retains the memory / calling-to-mind dimension that *sati*'s English translation as "mindfulness" largely loses. Toshikazu Arai has argued that *nenbutsu* is itself a kind of meditation — "the ultimate form of meditation," in his polemical phrasing — precisely because it preserves *anussati*. A Japanese reader sees 念 in 正念 and 念仏 and 妄念 and 一念 — and unless they have been trained in the relevant scholastic distinctions, the resonances bleed together.

### Part 5: The three-way (really five-way) collision

#### 5a. The map

| Context | 念 means | 無念 / no-念 means | Practical instruction |
|---|---|---|---|
| Theravāda (*sati*) | Non-floating retention; structuring of cognition; wholesome *cetasika* | Term not used | Establish and maintain *sati* (*satipaṭṭhāna*) |
| Chan / Zen (Huineng) | Thought / thought-moment | Non-fixation within thought; thoughts arise without grasping | Realize 無念 — transcend fixation |
| Chan / Zen (Linji, Dahui) | Conceptual mind to be cut through | "No-thought" as breakthrough | Concentrate *huatou* until 念 ruptures |
| Sōtō Zen (Dōgen) | Object-oriented thinking (思量) | 非思量: neither thinking nor not-thinking | Sit *shikantaza*; let non-thinking happen |
| Pure Land (Hōnen / Shinran) | Recollection / recitation of the Buddha | Term not used | Engage 念仏: say the Name |
| Modern mindfulness (Kabat-Zinn) | "Non-judgmental present-moment awareness" | Term not used | Notice neutrally |
| Modern Japanese popular | Often translated as 気づき (*kizuki*) | — | "Notice" / "realize" |

#### 5b. How the confusions compound

A Theravāda-trained reader encountering Zen 無念 will hear "no-mindfulness," an apparent abandonment of the practice. A Chan-trained reader encountering Theravāda's exhortation to cultivate 念 (*sati*) will hear "cultivate thought" — the opposite of what the Pāli intends. A Pure Land reader brings 念仏's recitative / memorial flavour to both. A reader of modern mindfulness literature — Kabat-Zinn, MBSR — comes equipped with "bare attention," which (per Sharf, Gethin, Dreyfus) is itself a 20th-century reformulation traceable through Nyanaponika's *The Heart of Buddhist Meditation* (Rider & Co., 1962) and behind that to Burmese *vipassanā* reform (Mahāsi Sayādaw and others; see Erik Braun, *The Birth of Insight: Meditation, Modern Buddhism, and the Burmese Monk Ledi Sayadaw* [University of Chicago Press, 2013]).

Because each register names itself with the **same character**, none of these readers is typographically warned that they are translating across a semantic chasm. The Chan novice who reads Bhikkhu Bodhi's "establish *sati*" and Huineng's "no 念" sees the same kanji twice and not unreasonably concludes one tradition contradicts the other. It does not — but reconciling them requires holding three different referents of 念 simultaneously in mind, which is itself a discipline.

#### 5c. Historical moments of compounding

**D.T. Suzuki** in *Zen and Japanese Culture* (originally published Japan, 1938; Princeton / Bollingen US ed. 1959) identifies *munen* (無念) with *muga* (無我), *mushin* (無心), and *musō* (無想), and treats the cluster as Zen's central insight: "*Mushin* [*wu-hsin*] or *munen* [*wu-nien*] is one of the most important ideas in Zen…. Enō (Huineng), the sixth patriarch of Zen, emphasizes *munen* (or *mushin*) as the most essential element in the study of Zen. When it is attained, a man becomes a Zen-man." The collapse of these terms into a single English "no-mind" or "no-thought" cluster — combined with Suzuki's rhetorical emphasis on the spontaneity of swordsmanship, tea, and haiku — gave the Anglophone world its first impression that Zen was anti-conceptual to its core. The Pāli-trained reader, encountering the same word "*sati* = mindfulness," then inferred the two traditions were doing different things; on Sharf's analysis they were also reading two different reformations.

**Nyanaponika Thera**'s *The Heart of Buddhist Meditation* (first published Rider & Co., London, 1962) defined *sati* operationally as "bare attention": "the clear and single-minded awareness of what actually happens to us and in us, at the successive moments of perception … without reacting to them by deed, speech or mental comment." **Jon Kabat-Zinn** — who founded MBSR in 1979 — gave the now-canonical formulation in *Wherever You Go, There You Are* (Hyperion, 1994), p. 4: "Mindfulness means paying attention in a particular way: on purpose, in the present moment, and non-judgmentally." Gethin (2011) and Sharf ("Is Mindfulness Buddhist? (and why it matters)," *Transcultural Psychiatry* 52, no. 4 [August 2015]: 470–484) show that this is a 20th-century reform of *sati*, not its commentarial definition; the *apilāpana* dimension drops out, and a new term is born under an old name.

**Thich Nhat Hanh** taught from within a Vietnamese Linji (Rinzai) lineage but drew on the *Satipaṭṭhāna* and *Ānāpānasati Suttas* of the Pāli canon. His *Miracle of Mindfulness* (1975) and the Plum Village tradition's "Fourteen Mindfulness Trainings" successfully bridged Theravāda *sati*-vocabulary into Zen practice in English — and in doing so blurred which 念 was in play. A meditator on a Plum Village retreat may be reading the Pāli *Satipaṭṭhāna Sutta* one hour and a Zen koan the next, with the assumption that "mindfulness" names the same practice in both. As Sharf has argued, that assumption needs scrutiny.

**Shunryū Suzuki**'s *Zen Mind, Beginner's Mind* (1970), Alan Watts's popularisations, and the broader Beat-era Zen import folded Zen "no-mind" vocabulary into the same English-language reservoir that MBSR would later draw on. By the 1990s, mainstream "mindfulness" carried Zen connotations (spontaneity, non-conceptuality, anti-intellectualism) it does not have in Theravāda sources, and Theravāda *sati* connotations (present-moment attention) it does not have in Chan.

**気づき (*kizuki*)** as a Japanese translation of "mindfulness" is the newest and most revealing layer. Japanese Wikipedia's article on サティ explicitly proposes both 念 and 気づき as Japanese equivalents of "mindfulness"; the Japanese title of Gunaratana's *Mindfulness in Plain English* uses 『気づきの瞑想』. *Kizuki* in everyday Japanese means "noticing," "realisation," even "epiphany"; it has no Buddhist textual pedigree. Adopting it as the rendering of "mindfulness" cuts across 念's Buddhist resonances and recodes *sati* as a generic perceptual quality — exactly the move Kabat-Zinn made in English with "awareness."

### Part 6: 非思量 as a resolution

Dōgen's 非思量 has structural features that none of the other positions has:

- It does **not** say cultivate 念 (the naïve *sati* reading) — that would still reify thought-as-object.
- It does **not** say eliminate 念 (the naïve 無念 reading) — Dōgen explicitly criticises this in *Zazenshin*.
- It does **not** say merely observe 念 from outside (the MBSR move) — that posits a watcher distinct from what is watched.
- It says: thinking and not-thinking are not the operative categories. The actuality of *zazen* is not located on that axis at all.

This converges, perhaps surprisingly, with what Gethin, Anālayo, and Dreyfus argue *sati* actually does in the Pāli sources: not a stripping of cognition bare, but a *structuring* of cognition such that recollection, present attention, and ethical orientation cohere. Jonardon Ganeri's reading of Buddhaghosa (*Attention, Not Self*, 2017) is congruent: attention in Buddhaghosa is the *form* of cognitive activity, not a content-neutral spotlight. Compared with Tsongkhapa's analytical meditation, which deliberately passes *through* conceptual content to reach a non-conceptual realisation, Dōgen's 非思量 names the stable state in which thought happens without being structured by the dualism cultivate / eliminate.

This is not to flatten the traditions into agreement — the soteriological frames remain very different — but to argue that if you want a category that names what *sati* and 無念 are both trying to do without inheriting the wrong half of either, 非思量 is a more disciplined option than "mindfulness" or "awareness."

### Part 7: Why this matters for contemporary practice

The 念 / 無念 confusion is the engine of two specific contemporary distortions:

**1. The "stop thinking" reading of Zen.** Because 無念 looks like "no thought," and because Suzuki-era exports emphasised spontaneity and the cessation of discriminative mind, lay readers infer that Zen practice's goal is mental blankness. The *Platform Sutra*'s own text refuses this reading — "thoughts arise but leave no trace," "to refrain from thinking of anything … is to be *dharma*-ridden, and this is an erroneous view" (Huineng). The confusion has nonetheless propagated through popular Zen literature for a century and validates an anti-intellectual reading the tradition itself does not support.

**2. The flattening of *sati* into "bare attention."** Because modern English "mindfulness" carries the present-moment-attention flavour but drops the retentive / recollective / ethical dimensions, Theravāda practitioners trained on MBSR-flavoured material may be receiving a Burmese-*vipassanā*-via-Nyanaponika reformulation of *sati* without knowing it. Gethin's, Dreyfus's, and Bodhi's interventions are precisely correctives to this: *sati* is not affect-neutral and not free-floating.

A more honest contemporary lexicon would distinguish: 念ₛ (*sati* / *smṛti*, retentive structuring); 念ₜ (Zen "thought-moment"); 念ₐ (*anussati* / *nenbutsu*, recollective-recitative); and 無念ₕ (Huineng's non-fixation). The graph 念 itself etymologically suggests "present-hearted" (今 + 心) — which is why "mindfulness" feels intuitively right — but the Abhidharmic meaning emphasises memory and structuring, not present-moment bareness. The character, as so often in cross-cultural Buddhist transmission, has been promising one thing and delivering another for fifteen hundred years.

## Recommendations

1. **In writing and teaching, never let 念 stand untranslated.** Always disambiguate: *sati*-念, "thought-instant"-念, *anussati*-念, *wunian*-念. The cost is awkwardness; the benefit is that readers stop hallucinating cross-traditional agreement and disagreement that the texts do not assert.

2. **Treat "mindfulness" as a 20th-century technical term, not a translation.** With Gethin, Dreyfus, Anālayo, and Sharf, recognise that current usage descends from Nyanaponika (1962) → Kabat-Zinn (MBSR program 1979; canonical definition 1994). Where the canonical-Abhidharmic content of *sati* matters (memory, ethical directionality, structuring of cognition), use *sati* itself or "retention," not "mindfulness."

3. **Read 無念 with Huineng's own gloss, not its surface negation.** 於念而不念 ("not to think even when involved in thought") and 於一切境上不染 ("to be unstained in all environments") are the operative definitions; "no-thought" in English is a calque that misleads. McRae's BDK translation (Numata, 2000) and Yampolsky's (1967) are both reliable; Sharf (2014) is the indispensable critical study.

4. **For practitioners moving between traditions, hold 非思量 as the bridge.** Dōgen's grammatical move solves more problems than either Theravāda's *sati*-cultivation rhetoric or popular Zen's no-mind rhetoric, if one is willing to take it as a structural rather than a mystical claim. Hee-Jin Kim's *Dōgen on Meditation and Thinking* (SUNY, 2007) and Kasulis's *Zen Action / Zen Person* are the best entry-points.

5. **Be wary of 気づき as a Japanese rendering of "mindfulness."** It severs the term from its Buddhist textual matrix and recodes *sati* as a generic cognitive event. In Japanese-language practice contexts, prefer 正念 with explicit disambiguation, and reserve 気づき for the everyday "noticing" sense.

**Benchmarks that would change these recommendations.** If new translation-history scholarship demonstrated that Tang Chan masters were systematically using 念 in the technical Abhidharmic *sati*-sense rather than the "thought-instant" sense (against Sharf's reading), the central thesis here would weaken. If philological work on Burmese *vipassanā* showed Mahāsi-style "bare attention" had a pre-modern Abhidharmic pedigree richer than Sharf and Braun claim, the modern-mindfulness-as-reform line would need softening. Neither has yet emerged.

## Caveats

- **The Dunhuang *Platform Sutra* is not the only *Platform Sutra*.** The Zongbao (宗寶) Yuan recension differs in wording from the 8th-century Dunhuang text Yampolsky translated, and the famous §26 line on 前念 / 後念 is extended in later editions to 前念著境即煩惱、後念離境即菩提. Readers citing 無念 should specify which recension. John McRae's BDK translation (Numata, 2000) uses the Zongbao text.

- **"*Sati* = memory only" is itself contested.** Anālayo (2003) argues that *sati* does not *literally* mean memory but "that which facilitates and enables memory"; the present-moment-awareness dimension is genuinely there in the canon, even if it has been overweighted in modern usage. Bryan Levman's "Putting *smṛti* back into *sati*" (*Journal of the Oxford Centre for Buddhist Studies* 13, 2017: 121–149) pushes back the other way. The right view is that the term is both retentive and present-attentive; the modern problem is the suppression of the first pole, not the affirmation of the second.

- **Sharf's reading of early Chan as "deconstructive" is influential but not universal.** Bernard Faure (*The Rhetoric of Immediacy*, 1991) and John McRae (*Seeing Through Zen*, 2003) read the early Chan moves in compatible but not identical terms; some scholars (e.g., some Sōtō historians) resist the framing that early Chan rejected seated meditation as such.

- **D.T. Suzuki and Nishida are now read more critically.** Sharf's 1995 "Buddhist Modernism and the Rhetoric of Meditative Experience" argues that the "Zen as direct experience" framework Suzuki popularised was itself a Meiji-era construction inflected by Nishida Kitarō's Kyoto School. This affects how much weight one should place on Suzuki's identifications of *munen* / *mushin* / *muga*.

- **Modern Pure Land scholarship debates whether 念仏 is "meditation."** Toshikazu Arai and others argue Shinran's *nenbutsu* is the ultimate meditation; many Jōdo Shinshū teachers explicitly reject the meditation framing. Both positions are live.

- **"Five-way collision" is rhetorical compression.** The traditions are internally diverse: there is no single "Theravāda" *sati* (Burmese *vipassanā* vs. Thai Forest tradition vs. Sinhala scholasticism differ), no single Zen 無念 (Caodong vs. Linji vs. Dōgen differ), no single Pure Land 念仏. The argument is that the character 念 conceals these differences from the eye of someone moving across the traditions; not that each tradition speaks with one voice.
